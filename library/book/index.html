Where the Wild Stays

A novella · Stuff So Sweet

When PhD wildlife biologist Andrea Ostrom tracks a stationary collar signal deeper into the Bitterroot wilderness than her supervisor would approve, she finds a man living in an abandoned ranger cabin that shouldn't be inhabited — a man who knows her name before she gives it, whose eyes are the wrong color for a human face, and who quietly admits to keeping the collar of the wolverine she came to find. As a hard winter closes the watershed around them, Andrea discovers what Kael is — the last surviving vidkar, a wolverine shifter whose mother was hunted down by government agents eleven years before — and what she is willing to risk to keep him from being found.

Chapter 1

The Cabin

The signal had been stationary for eleven days.

Andrea Ostrom checked her receiver again, frowning at the steady blip on the small screen. Gulo gulo, female F-114, collared eight months ago in the northern Bitterroot — a wolverine she'd named Maeve in her field notebook, against the advice of her supervisor, who maintained that naming research subjects was both unscientific and unprofessional. Andrea had told him that every male field biologist in her cohort named their bears, and he'd told her and that doesn't make it right.

She'd named the wolverine anyway.

Maeve hadn't moved in eleven days. Not the few hundred meters of foraging she usually logged in a day; not the restless circling of a denning female. Just the same coordinates, ping after ping, deep in a section of the Bitterroot where Andrea was not, strictly speaking, authorized to be.

She adjusted her pack. Checked the bear spray clipped at her chest. Checked, with a thumb that had begun to do this automatically, the holstered Glock 19 at her hip. She'd had the gun for two years. She'd never had to draw it on anything but paper.

The trail thinned. The lodgepoles closed in. The light went the particular blue of late November in high country — the hour the world starts breathing colder than your lungs.

The signal led off the marked trail.

She had three reasons to turn back: jurisdiction, weather, the fact that her supervisor had specifically forbidden any solo extensions of her research grid beyond the established transects. She had one reason to keep going: an animal in her catalog hadn't moved in eleven days, and there were six things that explained that, and five of them were dead.

She kept going.

The cabin came into view a quarter-mile later, in a clearing she hadn't expected and couldn't find on any of her topographic maps. Forest Service work, by the look of it — the old kind, hand-hewn cedar logs gone silver-grey with weather, a tin chimney patched at the seam, a porch sagging on one side. An abandoned ranger station, probably. The Bitterroot was full of them, decommissioned in the early 2000s when the agency consolidated its operations.

Smoke was coming from the chimney.

Andrea stopped at the edge of the clearing and went still. Not afraid — not yet. Listening. A scientist's first instinct: take stock before you move.

Smoke meant someone was here. Someone living rough — no road approached this cabin, not even an ATV trail. Someone who had brought everything he had on his back, the way she had. Someone who, if he had any sense at all, would not be glad to see a stranger walk out of the treeline.

She drew the Glock. Didn't chamber a round. Kept it against her thigh.

"Hello at the cabin," she called. Then, because it might matter: "I'm with the University of Montana. Wildlife biology. I'm tracking a collared animal."

The door opened.

A man stepped onto the porch.

He was tall. Six-two, maybe a little more. Dark hair longer than was fashionable, scraped back from a face that wasn't handsome so much as built — broad cheekbones, a jaw that had been broken once and reset clean, a winter beard coming in. He was wearing flannel and a wool jacket and boots that had been resoled by someone who knew how, and he stood very still on the porch in the way that careful animals stand still.

His eyes were the color of wet bark.

They were the wrong eyes for a human face.

She felt it before she could name it — that prickle along the back of her neck which, in fieldwork, was the only honest warning you ever got. The eyes were too steady. Too attentive. A man might look at a woman with that much focused attention only in two situations, and one of them was if he was deciding whether to kill her.

"You're a long way from your transects, Andrea Ostrom."

She didn't move. Didn't breathe, for a second.

"I'm sorry?" she said.

"Your permit. Posted on the trailhead board." His voice was lower than the words required, like he was used to keeping it down. "University of Montana, fieldwork in zones three and seven." He tilted his head a fraction. "This isn't zone seven."

She made herself breathe.

The trailhead was eleven miles south. She'd registered her grid-extension request there, signed her name on the public board. Of course. He'd been down for supplies, or — whatever it was men who lived in unauthorized cabins came down for. He'd read the board. Normal explanation. Sensible explanation.

She kept the Glock against her thigh.

"And you are?" she said.

"Kael."

He waited, watching her, as if expecting her to ask for a last name. When she didn't, the corner of his mouth moved — not a smile, exactly. The acknowledgment of something.

"You can put the gun away, Andrea Ostrom. I've had a lot of chances to hurt you on the way in. I'd have taken one already."

She didn't put the gun away.

"You knew I was coming."

"I heard you for the last hour." A pause. "You walk like a researcher. Heel-toe. Your boots have new laces. The straps on your pack haven't gone grey at the metal yet." He shrugged — a small movement, contained. "I had time to decide whether to be on the porch when you got here."

It was the kind of observation Andrea herself made about animals. She'd written a whole chapter of her dissertation about gait analysis in Mustela species. To hear it leveled at her, by him, was like watching her own training reflected in a window glass — and finding, somehow, that the window was a mirror in only one direction.

Her receiver pinged.

The collar.

She glanced down on instinct, training overriding caution — the signal was very strong now, much stronger than it had been from the trail. F-114. Maeve. Eleven days in the same square meter.

She looked back up at him.

"I'm tracking a collared wolverine," she said carefully. "The signal is coming from here."

He didn't react. Just held her eyes. Then, after a moment that lasted longer than a moment should, he said: "Yes."

He turned, went into the cabin, came back out. He was holding a wolverine collar in his hand. The leather strap was clean, but the band where the radio housing sat was dark with what could only be dried blood. He held it out, casually, as if it were any object. A coffee mug. A tool.

"I found her last week," he said. "She'd been caught in a trap that wasn't legal. Someone else's poacher. I couldn't save her." He stopped. Started again, softer. "I kept the collar because I knew somebody had been tracking her, and they'd come looking, and I'd want to tell them." He looked at her. "I'm telling you."

She crossed the clearing — slowly — and took the collar from his hand.

It was warm.

From the cabin, she told herself. From being held near his body. From the woodstove inside.

It did not feel warm the way a thing that had been near a stove felt warm.

"Where is she?" she said.

"I buried her behind the cabin. Two days ago, when the ground was soft enough."

"You buried her."

"She was a creature." He met her eyes again with those bark-colored, too-attentive eyes. "She deserved to be put under." He paused. "I can show you the place tomorrow, if you'd like. It's getting dark."

She looked up.

He was right. The light was going fast, the way it went at this elevation, like someone draining a glass into something larger. She was eleven miles from her base camp. She was outside her permit boundary. Her supervisor didn't know exactly where she was. Her satellite phone had three bars of battery left.

She had a Glock. She had a man in front of her who'd held the collar of an animal she'd been tracking and said the word creature the way someone might say child.

"I'll camp at the edge of the clearing," she said.

He inclined his head a fraction. As if he'd expected that answer. As if he might even, in some small way, have respected it.

"Do you have food?" he asked.

"Yes."

"There's a stack of split firewood by the woodshed. Take what you need. You shouldn't be cold tonight, Andrea Ostrom."

She set her tent at the edge of the clearing, eighty yards from his porch, behind a deadfall that would slow any approach but wouldn't block her sightlines to the cabin door. She ate a freeze-dried beef stroganoff without tasting it. She watched the smoke rise from his chimney and dissolve into the deepening sky.

He did not come out of the cabin again.

The collar — F-114, Maeve, eleven days dead — she put inside her tent, beside her sleeping bag. The receiver kept pinging steadily. She muted it.

She fell asleep at some point with the Glock under the rolled-up jacket she was using as a pillow, and slept fitfully, and woke once at — she checked her watch — twelve minutes after three in the morning, to the sound of something walking very softly in the snow outside her tent.

She didn't move.

The footfalls circled her tent. Stopped. Resumed. Circled again. They were not heavy — she'd heard bear sign before, and this wasn't bear. Quick, articulate steps. The gait of a predator that knew how to move in snow.

Soft snow muffled them but couldn't hide them.

She drew the Glock from beneath her jacket-pillow, very slowly, and waited.

The circling stopped.

The footfalls moved away — back toward the cabin, she thought, though she couldn't be sure. The night went still.

She did not sleep again.

At first light she unzipped her tent and stepped out into the new snow. Glock in her right hand. Receiver in her left.

The clearing was a clean white sheet. Her tracks coming in, yesterday. His porch — undisturbed, no boot prints leaving the cabin.

And, looped around her tent in a perfect, deliberate oval, the tracks of a wolverine.

Andrea crouched. Touched the edge of one print with a gloved finger. Measured it against her hand. Pad width almost five inches across. Stride length over half a meter.

Bigger than any wolverine in her catalog. Bigger than the published maximums for the species.

She looked back at the cabin.

The smoke from the chimney was just rising — a new fire, just started.

Her receiver chirped.

She looked down at the screen.

The signal — F-114, Maeve, the collar he had given her, the one that should now have been sitting on the floor of her tent beside her sleeping bag —

— was no longer pinging from her tent.

It was pinging from inside the cabin.

Chapter 2

The Wrong Creature

She did not break camp.

That was the first decision, made at the kitchen-counter level of her brain before the rest of her had quite finished arguing about it. If the collar was inside the cabin, the collar was inside the cabin. There was no version of leaving that returned her, professionally or personally, to a life she could explain.

She made coffee on her camp stove with hands that wanted to shake and did not. She drank it standing, watching the smoke from his chimney climb the morning sky in a thread so straight it looked drawn. She thought, with a clarity that surprised her, about the shape of the wolverine tracks looped around her tent. About the leather collar warm in her hand. About the way creature had sounded in his mouth.

When she walked to the cabin, she left the Glock in its holster and her hand near it.

He was outside. He had his back to her, splitting wood by the side of the cabin, his jacket and flannel and an undershirt all set aside on the porch rail. The morning was cold enough that her breath came in clouds and his didn't. Or — she watched more carefully — it did, but less. As if some part of his interior weather ran warmer than hers.

He didn't turn until she was twenty feet from him.

When he did, the maul was still in his hand. Not raised. Just held, the way some men hold a hammer when they're thinking about a problem. He set it down before he spoke.

"Andrea."

"Kael."

His chest was bare under the suspenders. She had not meant to look. Looking was something she did anyway, because she had been trained to take in information regardless of its category. His chest. The set of his shoulders. The pale flat of his stomach. The —

The scars.

They started at his right collarbone and ran in three parallel lines down across his ribs, ending somewhere below the band of his suspenders. Long. Deep. The kind of healed wound where the tissue had pulled tight and gone shiny. She had seen something like them on a sow bear once, and on a husky in Iditarod recovery. She had never seen anything like them on a human body.

He let her look. Didn't cover up. Didn't flinch.

"What did that to you," she said. It was not a question.

"Long time ago." He picked up his shirt and put it on, slowly. Not for modesty, she thought — for the cold. "Doing tracker work for the Service. There was an animal I didn't see in time."

"You said you'd been a tracker a long time ago."

"I did."

"Wolverine?"

He looked at her over his shoulder, buttoning the flannel. "Cougar."

She let that sit. It was a lie. She knew it was a lie because she had spent five years doing exactly this work, and she knew what claw marks at what stride did what damage. Cougar would have been four parallel lines, not three, and curved, not straight. These were the marks of an animal that had stood up to strike.

She let him have the lie because she wanted to see what he did with it.

"The collar's inside," she said.

"Yes."

"It moved during the night."

"It did."

She had not expected that. She had expected another lie. The truth — yes, it moved — was somehow harder to face than denial would have been. She put her hand on the porch rail. The wood was warm in the sun.

"How," she said.

He came up onto the porch with her. Stood close, but not crowding, his back against the rail in a way that put her between him and the open clearing. A defensive posture toward the world, not toward her. She catalogued it.

"You'll think I'm strange," he said.

"You are strange."

"I sleepwalk."

She turned her head and looked at him.

"I have, since I was a boy. Bad sometimes. I went out last night. I think I came back in with the collar." He shrugged, an apology of a movement. "I should have told you. I didn't want you to be afraid of me."

It was a better lie than the cougar. It even had a piece of truth in it — something in him had gone out in the night, and something had come back. She could feel it sitting underneath his words like the receiver pinging beneath snow.

"And the tracks around my tent?" she said.

"I don't know about tracks."

"There were wolverine tracks around my tent. A large male, by the size."

"Then you saw a wolverine, Andrea Ostrom. You should be glad. That's what you came here for."

She watched his face and his face gave her nothing. She thought of three further questions to ask, decided against all of them, and said instead: "The collar. I need it back. I have to log F-114's recovered remains, and I need to bury her properly with my supervisor's sign-off. I can't just have a collar appear in someone's cabin."

"I understand. I'll get it for you."

"And I need to see where you buried her."

"I'll show you. After noon. The light is better."

He brought the collar out, and she put it in a labeled ziplock in her pack, and they spent two hours splitting wood together because she did not know what else to do with the time. He gave her the maul once she'd proven, in the first few swings, that she could split a round without splitting her own foot. He worked beside her with an axe. They didn't talk. The sound of the work was companionable in a way that made her uneasy.

At noon they ate, separately, on opposite ends of the porch. He ate what looked like dried elk, taken from a pouch. He drank water out of a tin cup. She ate a granola bar and tried not to watch him eat.

He ate with the side of his mouth.

After lunch he walked her to the place behind the cabin where he'd said he buried her. It was a small mound of stones in a sheltered spot under a fir. The stones were neatly laid. The dirt beneath them was darker than the surrounding soil — recently turned.

"I'm sorry," he said, looking at the mound. "I should have left her where she was. So you could log it."

"It's fine," she lied. "I'll write the field note."

She stayed there a moment longer than she meant to, looking at the small cairn. Whoever had built it had built it with care.

When she stood, she saw it. Not on the cairn itself — beside it. In the soft dirt at the foot of the fir. A single wolverine print. Bigger than the ones around her tent. Not part of any pattern. Just the one print, deep, like the animal had stood there a while.

She looked at him.

"I sleepwalk," he said again, quietly. "It happens here too."

The clouds came in faster than she had estimated.

She had finished her field note and was packing to start back to base camp when the first hard gust took her tent down to the snow. The temperature dropped twenty degrees in an hour. The sky went the slate-grey of a major front. By the time she'd repacked her gear and made it back across the clearing, the snow was coming sideways and she couldn't see the treeline.

He opened the door before she knocked.

"It'll be a bad one," he said. "Come in. The bed's yours. I'll sleep by the fire."

"Kael —"

"You'll freeze." He held the door wider. "Come in, Andrea Ostrom. I won't touch you."

She came in.

The cabin was one room and a loft. A woodstove in the corner roaring. A single iron bed against the far wall, made with grey wool blankets and a single white pillowcase that had been laundered to softness. A table. Two chairs. A shelf of books — she saw Darwin's Voyage and a paperback Mary Oliver and what looked like a French dictionary. A rifle on hooks above the door. A row of cast-iron pans hanging from beams. No photographs. No clutter. No personal objects of any kind, except for one — a child's wooden carving of a small four-legged animal, set on the windowsill above the sink.

He saw her notice it and said nothing.

He put coffee on. He pulled a folded oilcloth from a chest and laid it on the floor between the woodstove and the wall, then a wool bedroll on top of it. He banked the fire and tended it and brought her a tin mug of coffee that tasted exactly like her father's had tasted, which was to say strong enough to register on the receiver.

He did not look at her except when he handed her the mug.

She slept in his bed in her clothes with the Glock under the pillow and the Maeve collar in its ziplock at the foot of the mattress. He slept on the floor near the woodstove. The storm rattled the cabin and the woodstove ticked and somewhere in the high country a tree came down with a sound like distant thunder.

At three in the morning, she woke to a draft.

She lay still. The fire had burned down to coals. There was just enough light from the woodstove to make out the shape of the room. The oilcloth and bedroll on the floor were empty. The door was open. Snow was drifting in across the threshold, building in small fingers on the wooden floor.

She lay there for a long time, listening, not moving.

There were no footfalls. There was only the storm outside, and the open door, and the cold breathing in.

She drew the Glock from beneath the pillow and held it across her chest under the blanket. She did not get up to close the door. Something — some old animal piece of her, the same piece that had named Maeve — told her that to close the door before he came back would be a kind of trespass.

She waited.

She must have slept again at some point, because the next thing she knew the door was closed and the fire was built up and there was the smell of bacon. Grey light at the window. Kael was at the woodstove with his back to her, wearing his flannel and suspenders, his hair wet. He had built the fire and he was making her breakfast.

He didn't turn around. He said, without looking at her: "Sorry about the door. I get cold sometimes. I have to go out."

She thought about this for a long moment.

"You went out in that," she said. It was not a question.

"Yes."

"In a blizzard."

"Yes."

"Why."

He turned then, holding a cast-iron pan in one hand and a fork in the other. The hair at his temples was wet and dark. His cheeks were flushed from cold or from heat — she could not tell. His eyes, in the grey light, were the same wet-bark color as before, and they were the same eyes that had looked at her over a maul in the morning, and they were the same eyes that had watched her over the rim of a coffee cup, and they were the wrong eyes for a human face.

"Because I had to," he said.

He set a plate in front of her. Bacon and fried eggs and a piece of brown bread with butter melting into it. The eggs were perfectly cooked. The bread was hot.

"Eat," he said. "We'll go back to your base camp when the storm breaks. I'll walk you down. You shouldn't be out alone after a storm like that."

She ate.

She did not ask him where he had been. She did not ask him why his cheeks were flushed or why his hair was wet or what came in with the cold. She ate, and watched him at the woodstove, and thought:

You are the wrong creature.

You are the creature I came here to find.

Chapter 3

Tracks in the Snow

The storm cleared overnight.

She woke in his bed to the silence that follows weather — that bowl-shaped quiet, the world full of new snow. Outside the small window above the table, the clearing was a different country. Knee-deep and clean. The pine boughs bent low under it. The smoke from the chimney rose now in a fat lazy column, no wind at all.

Kael was already up. He had stoked the fire and was lacing his boots at the door. He had a long thin pack at his feet — small enough for a day trip, big enough for everything he needed.

"I'll walk you to your base camp," he said when he saw her stir. "You won't get a sledge through this, but you'll be okay on snowshoes."

"You don't have to."

"I do." He did not elaborate. He set a tin of coffee on the table beside her and began the careful business of banking the woodstove for an absence.

She drank the coffee. She watched him work. He moved through the cabin with the economy of a man who had done every chore in it ten thousand times. He emptied the basin. He put away the dishes. He checked the latch on the back door — the door that opened toward the small cairn of stones — and laid a length of split cedar against it on the inside. He put the rifle in a leather sling and put the sling over his shoulder. He looked once around the room.

He looked, she noticed, at the wooden carving on the windowsill. A long look. The carving was about the length of his thumb, smoothed by handling. A four-legged animal she couldn't identify at this distance, the legs too short for a dog, the body too long for a cat.

Then he turned out the lamp and they left.

The trail down through the lodgepoles was a different trail in the new snow. He went first, breaking, his snowshoes laying a track she could step into. She followed three paces behind. They didn't talk. She watched the back of his neck, where his hair had grown long enough to curl over the collar of his jacket. She watched the way his rifle rode the strap. She watched her own breath form clouds in the cold air, and his, and noticed again that his came less often and less white. As if he was burning less of what she was burning.

At a switchback he stopped to wait for her.

"Why don't you ask," he said.

She caught up.

"Ask what?"

"Whatever it is you're not asking."

The morning was very still. The lodgepoles around them held the new snow on every branch like a held breath. She looked at him. He had pushed his hood back, and a few strands of his hair were stuck to the corner of his mouth from the wind.

"All right," she said. "Why don't you eat with both sides of your mouth?"

He laughed.

It was a real laugh — quick, almost surprised, the way it sometimes comes out of people who haven't laughed for a long time. It transformed his face for a half-second into something younger than he was. Then it was gone, and he was watching her again with that wary attention, but the corners of his eyes had softened.

"I have an old jaw injury," he said. "It got broken when I was twenty-three. Doesn't open all the way on the right side anymore."

"Cougar?"

"No."

He started walking again. She matched his pace.

After a while she said: "Will you tell me what?"

"My mother died," he said. It was the kind of sentence offered as if it were an answer. "It was a hard time. There was an accident."

"I'm sorry."

"It was a long time ago."

"How long?"

A pause. "Eleven years."

She let that walk with them, downhill, through the new snow, for a quarter of a mile before she said anything else.

"My mother is still alive," she said eventually. "She doesn't approve of fieldwork. She doesn't approve of the Glock. She thinks I'm hiding from a life by living in a tent. She's not entirely wrong."

"What are you hiding from?"

She'd asked for it. She gave him the answer.

"I was engaged. Two years ago. Three months out from the wedding, he took a position at Stanford. He thought I would come. I'd just been awarded the grant for this — for the Bitterroot. He told me to defer it. I told him to defer Stanford. He went anyway." She kicked a chunk of crust off her snowshoe. "We were together six years. I lost more of my life with him than I have left. So I decided the next six were going to be the wolverines."

"That seems like a good trade."

She laughed — once, sharp. "He thinks I'm punishing him."

"Are you?"

"No. He doesn't matter that much. I'm just — I'm trying not to be the kind of woman who picks a person over the work."

He thought about that.

"That's a hard kind of woman to be."

"Yes."

They didn't talk for a long time after that.

Her base camp was a flat shelf above the creek, a wall tent and a small canvas vestibule and a stack of supplies on a tarped pallet. She'd set it up to last the winter. The fence she had built around the food cache to keep grizzlies out had — she saw immediately — come down on one side, the snow load too much for the cordage she'd used. The bear-proofing was effectively off-line. The food was still inside the cache but two big snow-blown sacks of trash had broken loose and were strewn against the cache wall.

He walked the perimeter once before saying anything.

"You can't leave this tonight," he said. "If a sow comes through she'll work right into it."

"I know."

"I'll help you fix it."

"You don't —"

"I will help you fix it. The light's already going. You won't get it done before dark."

She wanted to argue. There was a part of her — the part that had told her fiancé to defer Stanford, the part that lived in the Glock at her hip, the part that did not want to be helped — that wanted very badly to argue.

She didn't.

"Thank you," she said.

He could work. That was the simple, surprising thing about it. He could work the way her father had been able to work, when she was a girl on the ranch — that easy fluency with rope and post and tarp and lashing. He didn't waste motion. He showed her one knot, twice, and she had it. He spliced new cordage into the fence line. He set anchors deeper. He propped the cache with two new uprights. When she dragged the trash sacks down to the creek to weight them in the deep pool and freeze them out of bear interest, he carried the other one without being asked.

They worked through dusk and into the first real dark. When the fence was done they ate, sitting in her vestibule by the small camp stove. He'd brought jerky from the cabin; she added crackers and a tin of sardines, and they shared a chocolate bar she'd been saving.

He sat across the camp stove from her with his long legs folded and his hands in his lap. He looked, she noticed, content. Not happy — that was a different country, somewhere he might not have been to in a long time. But unstrained. Less watchful than he'd been on the porch the first night. As if he'd let some piece of himself relax that he didn't relax for everyone.

She didn't want him to leave.

That was the second thing she realized that day that surprised her.

"You should stay tonight," she said. She had not planned to say it. "It's a hard walk back to the cabin in the dark."

He looked at her over the stove.

"I have a second sleeping bag," she said. "And the vestibule is wide enough for two if you don't mind being close to the stove."

He thought.

"All right."

She tried her sat phone before bed. The screen lit weakly, then died. She tried it again. Nothing.

"My phone's dead," she said, frowning at it.

He was in the vestibule, threading the laces of his boots through the doorway so they'd stay dry. He looked over.

"It charged this morning?"

"Three bars yesterday afternoon."

He came over. Took it from her. Held it for a moment, looking at it the way some men look at a knot they intend to untie. Then he handed it back.

"They don't always like high country," he said, as if he were finishing a thought. "I've seen them go dead before, the satellite phones, in cold weather."

"It wasn't that cold."

"No," he agreed, mildly. "It wasn't."

She looked at him.

"Sleep," he said. "I'll be just here."

She slept.

She woke to the smell of coffee and to his quiet movement just outside the canvas wall. The grey was barely up. Through the unzipped flap she could see him in the snow, splitting more of her firewood — she'd had a small stack from before; he was doubling it.

She watched him work for a moment from the warmth of her bag. The economy of motion. The straight, easy swing. The way the breath came out of him less white than hers.

He had stayed.

He could have walked back. The dark wouldn't have stopped him. She knew this with a clarity that did not require evidence: the dark wouldn't have stopped him. He had stayed because he wanted to be here when she woke.

She thought about her ex, in his white Stanford office, in the future she had not gone toward. She thought about her supervisor, eleven miles south, who would in due course want to know why she had not transmitted a status check yesterday.

She thought about the soft carving of a small four-legged animal on a windowsill.

She got up. She dressed. She came out of the tent.

"Good morning," she said.

He turned, the maul in mid-swing, and lowered it. He looked at her the way he had looked at her, briefly, in the cabin two nights ago when she'd said I'll camp at the edge of the clearing — with something that might have been respect, and might have been longing, and might have been both.

"Good morning, Andrea Ostrom," he said.

He went back to the wood.

She stood there in the cold, watching, and let it sit inside her like a held coal.

Chapter 4

The Bond

He should have left. He didn't.

The first day after the storm she expected him to walk back to the cabin once the chores were done, and when noon came and went and he was still there — repairing a slow leak in her tarped pallet that she had not, herself, noticed — she expected him to leave by dusk. He did not. He ate with her again at her stove. He slept again in the vestibule.

On the second morning he said: "I'll bring a few things down from the cabin. Be back by dark."

He brought, by dark, a coil of better rope, a smaller cast-iron skillet, a jar of honey, a wool blanket, and a leg of venison wrapped in butcher paper. He set them at the door of her vestibule and went off without explanation to gut and rig a snare for hare along the willows where the creek bent. She did not ask him to.

On the third morning he found her standing at the creek staring at the pool where she'd weighted the trash sacks. She was watching the ice form. He came up beside her quietly.

"Tell me you don't always stay this far out alone," he said.

"I do, actually. The other camps are sixty miles up."

"You've done this how long?"

"Six months in. Six to go."

"And in the second six you'll be on the other side of the watershed."

"Yes."

He looked at the ice for a while.

"It's not safe," he said.

"I know."

He didn't press it. He went back to the camp.

He started staying at the camp. Not every night — some nights he disappeared up the trail at dusk and was back at dawn with snow on his boots and his breath running less white than hers — but most nights. He never asked. She never invited him. The way it happened was: he would do something useful at the end of the afternoon, and the end of the afternoon would shade into the evening, and the evening into the cold, and he would already be there when the cold dropped, and at some point in the early dark she would say do you want some coffee and he would say yes and that was the night decided.

He fixed the door of her vestibule. He set new anchors on the wall tent. He left small things at the edge of her camp without telling her — a deer hindquarter hung from a branch where she'd find it on her morning walk. A pile of split kindling. A pair of dry socks she hadn't asked for. A single pinecone in the doorway, perfect, as if it had been chosen.

She made notes about her wolverine grid. She filed her status checks to her supervisor by the radio when she could get a signal from the high meadow above her camp, which she had to ski to in the new snow. She did not mention Kael in any of them. She did not allow herself, when she came back to camp in the early dark with the radio still warm against her chest, to admit that this was deliberate.

The receiver in her tent never pinged. F-114's collar had gone dark in the way collars went dark when there was no one to wear them anymore.

Eleven days had become fifteen, then twenty.

She started to imagine the spring.

It was the seventh evening of him sleeping in her vestibule when he asked if he could come inside the wall tent.

It had been a hard day. A real cold front, twenty below at the high meadow, both of them out longer than they should have been. They'd come back to her camp at full dark and both eaten at the stove without much talking. She'd lit the small kerosene lantern she used inside the tent on hard nights, because the stove vestibule was the dangerous heat and the tent was where the body actually rested.

She was already inside the tent, in her bag, with the lantern between them on the floor near the entrance, when he ducked his head into the flap.

"I'm sorry," he said. "I can't feel my hands. The wind got up on me. May I sit by your lantern for a few minutes? The vestibule is too cold."

He had splints of ice in his hair. His lips were grey at the edges. She was angry at him, briefly, for being cold for too long — and then angry at herself for being a person who could be made angry by someone else's cold.

"Come in," she said. "Take off your jacket. Put your hands inside the bag."

He did. He shrugged the jacket off and folded himself onto the bag beside her — long legs careful in the small space — and put his hands inside the open mouth of her sleeping bag, against her stomach.

His hands were so cold she gasped.

"I'm sorry," he said again.

"Don't be sorry. Be warmer."

She put her hands over his. Hers were warm; his were ice. She held them there. He sat very still beside her, his shoulder almost against hers, his hands flat against her belly through the thermal of her base layer. The lantern made a small yellow island of light around them. The tent walls breathed in and out very slightly in a low wind.

For a long time neither of them spoke.

She could feel his hands gradually finding heat. She could feel his pulse in the inside of his wrists, against her ribs. She could feel his breath at her shoulder, slower than her own.

"Andrea," he said.

She turned her head.

His face was very close. Close enough that she could see the way the lantern light made his bark-colored eyes look, briefly, the color of something fired in a kiln — copper. Close enough that she could see the small scar at his lower lip, the one that disappeared into his beard. Close enough that she could see the way he was looking at her, which was the way he had been looking at her in some part of himself since the porch.

She kissed him.

She did not think about it. She did not weigh it. She did not run through the catalog of every reason she should not. She put her mouth against his and his hands flexed against her belly and his mouth opened under hers immediately, hungrily, like a man who had been thirsty in the wilderness for a long time.

He tasted like coffee and cold air and something else — something she could not name. Something that was not entirely the inside of a man's mouth. Like winter. Like spruce sap. Like blood iron.

She let it scare her and went on kissing him.

He made a low sound in his throat that was not a human sound. Not entirely. Closer to a hum than a moan, and beneath it some other register — a vibration she felt through her ribcage where his hands rested. His mouth opened further, and his teeth — she felt the edges of them, not against her tongue but at the corner of her lip, and she felt them scrape, lightly. Twice. Three times. As if he were learning the shape of her mouth.

The third time, one of his teeth — one of them, on the left side of his mouth — caught the soft swell of her lower lip and pressed, and the pressure was wrong. It was too much pressure for a human tooth in a tender place. It was the pressure of a tooth that knew exactly how much pressure to use to hold and not break.

She pulled back.

Half an inch. Not a flinch — a question.

He pulled back further. Six inches. A full foot. His eyes had gone black in the lantern light. Not narrowed. Black — the pupil and iris fused into one wide dark plate.

He looked at her with a horror she had not seen on a person's face before.

"I'm sorry," he said. His voice was rougher than it had been. "I'm sorry, Andrea — I'm sorry."

"Kael —"

He pulled his hands out of the bag and stood — bent under the tent ceiling, awkward — and was out through the flap before she could finish his name. She heard him pulling his jacket on in the vestibule. She heard the stove being banked. She heard him stop at the edge of the camp.

"Stay inside tonight," he said through the canvas. His voice was tight. "Please."

"Kael, come back."

"I'll come back tomorrow."

"Kael —"

"Please."

She heard him walking off into the snow.

He did not crash through it. He did not stumble in the cold. He walked away in long, easy strides — quicker, soon, than a man's stride. She listened to it lengthen. She listened to the rhythm of four footfalls fall into the rhythm of something else. She did not put her face out of the tent to see what was leaving. She was, for the first time in her life, afraid of the answer to a question she could resolve in two seconds with her eyes.

She lay in her bag with the lantern still lit between her and the door for a long time.

Eventually she touched her lip.

The tooth had not broken the skin. It had left only a slight indentation. The way teeth feel when they have only ever been a kiss. The way teeth feel when something else was tucking them in carefully, with great care, so that they could not do what they were made to do.

She breathed.

In the morning there were tracks around the tent again.

A perfect deliberate oval. Pad width almost five inches.

This time, at the edge of the oval where it came closest to the tent door, there was a long single line. As if the animal had stood there a long time. As if, where the rest of the tracks were measured and circling, this single set was the place it had wanted to come closer and could not.

Andrea crouched. Touched the print with her glove.

She did not cry. She felt something else — closer to a kind of grief, or a kind of recognition.

She stood up. Looked toward the upper trail, the way he had gone.

"I'm not afraid of you," she said into the cold morning. She said it quietly, but she said it out loud. "I'm not afraid of you, Kael."

The forest did not answer.

The forest, she had begun to suspect, was listening.

Chapter 5

What He Is

He did not come back the next day.

Or the day after.

By the third day, Andrea had begun to admit to herself, in the small interior way one admits a thing only to the bone of one's chest, that he might not come back at all. That the kiss had broken something he could not let her see again. That the man who came back at dawn carrying a deer hindquarter had decided, in some private interior of himself, that he was a danger to her, and that the kindness he could do her was a kindness of absence.

She also knew, by the third day, that she would not let that be his decision.

She packed light. The Glock. Two days of food. Snowshoes, headlamp, a thermal camera she'd been using to log nocturnal mustelid activity. The receiver. She made a sat-phone call from the high meadow — pleaded weather for a status delay, said she was tracking a high-priority signal — and started up the trail to his cabin.

The lodgepoles had filled in with new snow since the storm. The trail he had broken for her had drifted nearly closed. She had to break it again, the slow steady work of breaking trail in deep snow, the work that takes you out of your own head because your head cannot afford to wander. She liked it. The cold sat properly in her lungs. The receiver, muted at her hip, occasionally pinged with the static of the high-frequency range — environmental noise, mountain hum, the radio leaks of all the human and animal things alive on the watershed.

She reached the cabin at three in the afternoon.

It was empty.

The fire was out — not banked, out, gone cold for days. The door was closed but not locked. The cabin was still tidy in the way he kept it tidy, but the small carving of the four-legged animal was gone from the windowsill. The bedroll on the floor was rolled and tied as if for travel. A pair of his boots was missing from beside the door. The leather sling for the rifle was on its hook, empty. The rifle was gone.

She stood in the middle of the cold room for a long minute, listening to the house breathe.

She walked the perimeter once. No fresh boot tracks anywhere outside the door, just the old set fading under three days of new snow. He had not come back here from his last leaving.

At the edge of the clearing — the place behind the cabin where the small cairn of stones marked F-114's grave — she found the carving.

It was lying on the cairn, set carefully on the highest stone. As if returned to a place from which it had come.

She picked it up. Turned it in the light. She had not been close enough before to see what it was. Now, in her gloved hand, she saw clearly: a small, long-bodied four-legged animal with a thick stubby tail, the head almost too small for the body, the legs short and powerful. The hands of whoever had carved it had been good. The proportions were right.

It was a wolverine.

She stood there in the deepening afternoon holding a piece of wood that someone had carved into a wolverine and had set on a cairn of stones above another wolverine. She did not have to ask why. She knew, with the same quiet certainty she had felt at every other turn of this country, that what she held had been carved as a memory.

She put it in her pocket.

She went looking for him.

She found his trail at the edge of the clearing. Not human boots. The wolverine track, the big one, the one that had circled her tent.

She had been trained to follow this kind of trail. She did, methodically, taking note as she went. The animal had moved with purpose, not foraging. Stride length variable — sometimes the trail of an animal running, sometimes the trail of an animal walking. Once, in a stand of fir, she found a place where the snow was flattened in a wide circle and the trail simply stopped — and then started again twenty feet away, the same large prints, walking on as if the animal had paused to rest.

She did not let herself think yet.

The trail led up toward a high meadow she had passed but not entered on her first hike in — a bowl-shaped opening in the lodgepoles a quarter-mile above the cabin, the kind of place a predator would use for vantage. She circled below it carefully on the lee side. She found, at the edge of the meadow, on a low log, a pair of his boots and a folded set of clothes — flannel, jeans, his wool jacket. The clothes were carefully folded, not torn. The boots had been set neatly side by side.

She stood and looked at them for a long time. The afternoon light was beginning to drop into the blue hour.

She set up the thermal camera in a hide of broken pine boughs at the edge of the meadow, fifty yards from the clothes, facing the bowl. She crawled into the hide and got under her bivy and lay very still in the snow. She watched the small black-and-white screen of the camera. The world bled grey, the snow lukewarm, the trees lukewarm, the distant cabin chimney cold. No heat signatures.

She waited.

She waited an hour. The afternoon dropped into proper dark. Her breath formed small clouds inside the bivy. The receiver, half-buried in her pack beside her, occasionally chirped at the upper edge of its range.

At the seventy-second minute, on the camera screen, a bright signature crossed into the bowl from the upper end.

It was huge.

She had calibrated this camera for the upper end of the wolverine species mass — twenty-five, twenty-eight kilos at the outside. The animal on her screen blew through that. She could see the rough estimate of mass in the heat signature: fifty-five kilos. Maybe sixty. Built low and broad and heavy through the chest. It moved on four legs at a easy trot, the gait of an animal completely at home. It crossed the bowl, headed for the log where the clothes were folded, stopped six feet from them.

It sat.

It sat there a long time. Andrea did not move inside the bivy. She did not breathe. On the screen the heat signature shifted, lay down, rolled briefly, sat back up — animal restlessness. It put its nose into the air and held it there.

She knew before it happened. The hairs on her own forearms had gone up.

The animal stood. It walked to the log. It lowered its great heavy head into the folded clothes.

And then —

It was less like watching a transformation than like watching a perspective shift. The heat signature did not so much change as resolve. The shape of the animal pulled in, lengthened, and where it had been on four legs there was, slowly, the heat signature of a man kneeling in the snow. A man on his knees, naked, his back to her in the dark of the meadow, his arms hanging at his sides.

The animal was gone. He was there.

Andrea did not breathe.

On the camera screen, his head came up. His chin lifted toward the line of pines where her hide was. He held still.

Then he stood.

She watched the heat signature unfold to his full height. She watched him put his hands at his sides. She watched him not put any clothes on. She watched him walk through the snow in a straight line toward her hide.

She watched him walk fifty yards in the cold without dressing.

When he was twenty feet away, he stopped. He spoke. His voice was low, but it carried in the silence of the bowl.

"Andrea."

She did not answer. She kept her hand on the Glock under her bivy. She watched him through the camera, and through a small clear gap in the boughs over her head.

"I can smell you." There was no anger in it. Only a long slow ache, like a man saying a word he had not been able to say in a long time. "Please don't run."

She had thought, in the long minutes of waiting, that she would feel terror. She did not. She felt something stranger — a kind of ringing clarity, the same clarity she had felt looking at the wolverine prints around her tent on the first morning. As if every previous puzzle of the country had just opened a small clean window onto an answer.

She lowered the camera.

She set it aside.

She pushed back the bivy and sat up out of the snow.

Twenty feet from her, in the blue moonless dark, his shape resolved into a man. Tall. Naked. Steam coming off his shoulders in small white curls. His eyes — even in this light she could see them — black.

She got up to her knees.

"Don't run," he said again. His voice was almost a whisper. "I won't come closer."

"I'm not running."

He took a step. Stopped himself.

"You'll be cold," she said. Her voice had come out steady. She was surprised by it. "Your clothes are on the log."

He looked over his shoulder toward the log. He looked back at her.

"I should put them on?"

"Yes. Please."

He went. He moved beautifully naked in the cold, with the same easy economy he moved in clothes, his back to her, his shoulders broad and pale in the dark. The scars across them were visible even at this distance. He dressed in three minutes — the long underwear first, then the jeans, then the flannel, then the jacket. He sat down on the log to put on his boots. He laced them.

He did not look at her while he dressed.

When he was dressed, he stood, and turned to her, and stood with his hands open at his sides as if to show he was not hiding anything.

"You weren't supposed to follow me," he said. It was not an accusation. It was almost an apology.

"I know."

"I should have left further from your camp."

"You should have come back to me," she said. "I would have followed you here whether you wanted me to or not."

He closed his eyes briefly.

"Andrea —"

"Walk me to the cabin," she said. She was on her feet now, brushing snow from her pants. Her hands were not steady, but her voice was. "It's getting cold and I haven't eaten since noon and you've been in the snow for three days. Walk me to the cabin and build us a fire and tell me what you are."

He opened his eyes.

In them, in the blue-dark of the high meadow, she saw the man on the porch with the maul. She saw the man who had brought her bacon at dawn. She saw the boy who had carved a small wolverine for a mother he had lost, and she saw the wolverine who had walked at the edge of her camp in a careful, deliberate oval, and she saw the creature who had pressed his mouth to hers in a lantern-lit tent and tucked his teeth away with great care so as not to do what they were made to do.

She saw the wrong eyes for a human face, and she saw, behind them, the right ones.

"All right," he said.

He came across the snow and offered her his arm.

She took it.

They walked down to the cabin together in the dark.

Chapter 6

The Last of His Kind

He built a fire.

He moved through the dark cabin without lighting a lamp — opened the woodstove, scraped the cold ash into the bucket, set kindling on a single twist of paper, struck a match. The kindling caught at the first try. He fed it slowly, the way he did everything. He did not look at her while he worked.

She sat at the table in her jacket and watched him.

When the fire was steady he stood and lit the kerosene lantern and turned and faced her across the small room.

"What I am," he said.

She nodded.

He sat down opposite her. Put his hands on the table. Worn hands. Long fingers. The knuckles a little large for a man his age.

"My mother's people called us vidkar," he said. "I don't know what the word meant in their old language. She told me it just meant 'the ones in the trees.' She said we'd been in these mountains as long as the wolverines had. We die when they die. She told me there had only ever been a few of us, and we'd been smart enough to stay rare, and we'd lasted into the modern world by living the way wolverines lived — alone, high, and cold."

He paused. The light from the woodstove was on the side of his face. The bark-color of his eyes was, in firelight, almost gold.

"I was born in 1991. I was nineteen when she taught me to shift on purpose. Before that it was — accidents. Fevers. She kept me out of school. She taught me what I needed to know in the woods. I went into the Forest Service when I was twenty-one, did tracker work because I was good at it, because I could go places other men couldn't go, and because the badge gave me a reason to be in the country I needed to be in. She kept living high. I'd come up to her every weekend."

He looked at his hands.

"She was killed when I was twenty-three. Eleven years ago."

Andrea did not move.

"She was killed by men. They weren't poachers. They had government plates. The men knew what she was. I don't know how they knew." He looked up. "It wasn't a hunt. It was a removal. Like you might remove a thing that wasn't supposed to exist."

"I'm sorry."

"They tried for me after. I was hurt." He did not gesture to the scars. "I survived. I left the Service. I went off the maps. I came to this cabin and I have been here for eleven years. They have looked, twice or three times, in the years since. Small planes mostly. A black SUV at the western trailhead two summers ago. I don't think they're certain I'm here anymore. I have tried to stay — small. Unsmellable to the kind of people who go looking for a thing like me."

He watched her face.

"That's what I am, Andrea. The last of a thing your species' science hasn't found because we've been good for a long time at not being found. There may be a cousin or two of mine still alive somewhere — Alberta, the Yukon — but I haven't smelled one in eleven years. And the men who killed my mother were thorough."

She made herself breathe.

She had been a scientist for ten years. She had a doctoral committee waiting on her work. She had spent her whole adult life believing that Gulo gulo was the largest extant member of the Mustelidae, that no human-mammal chimera existed outside of folklore, that biology was a series of testable hypotheses and there were no wolves in the night that did not eventually consent to be photographed.

She had also, in the past four days, photographed something that did not exist.

"How does it work," she said. Her voice came out steady. She had asked the question as a scientist. Asking it as a scientist was the only protection she had. "How does it work biologically. Cellularly. Is it metabolic. Is it genetic — is it a switching of expression — is it —"

He almost smiled. The corner of his mouth.

"I don't know," he said. "My mother didn't either. We didn't have anyone to study us. We knew what worked. We didn't know why."

"What works."

"Calorie load. Body temperature. I have to eat — a lot, both as a man and as the other. I sleep more than other men. I don't get sick. I age slower than I think I should. I shift faster when I'm angry or when I'm afraid. The other form is — easier when I haven't been in it for a while. The longer I'm in it, the harder it is to come back."

"How long is the longest."

He looked away.

"Two weeks."

She put her hand over her mouth. He waited.

"Will you let me write about this?" she said, knowing as she said it that she would not. Asking anyway. Knowing he would understand that she was asking and refusing in the same breath.

"No."

"I know."

"I know you do."

She put her hand back on the table. The carving — the small wooden wolverine she had picked up from the cairn — was in her pocket. She took it out and set it on the table between them.

"You carved this."

"My mother did."

She had not expected to make a sound. She did. A small one — a breath catch — and Kael's eyes softened at the corners in a way that she understood, now, was him controlling his own face.

"She made it for me when I was four. She told me to keep it close, because if anything ever happened to her I should remember to be small. Be small. Be careful. Don't be the kind of thing that the world feels."

He reached across the table and touched the carving with one finger. He did not pick it up.

"I should not have left it on her cairn," he said. "That was wrong. I was — not thinking clearly. I went out and stayed out. I am sorry."

"Why did you stay out."

He looked up.

"Because I kissed you," he said. "And I almost — and I am dangerous, Andrea, you do not know what I am yet, you have only seen the easy part —"

"Kael."

"— and the longer I stayed away from you, the easier it was to be the other one, because the other one does not — does not need to keep his teeth careful for anyone — and I needed not to need anything for a few days —"

"Kael."

He stopped.

She slid the carving toward him.

"Pick it up," she said.

He did. He held it in his palm. He did not close his hand around it.

"You are not dangerous to me," she said. "You are dangerous to people who would try to take you. You are dangerous to whoever killed your mother. You are not dangerous to me. I saw you tuck your teeth away. I felt you tuck your teeth away. You did not do anything you cannot take back."

His jaw worked.

"I am dangerous to you," he said, "because everything that has ever wanted to come near me has had to die for it. You don't understand that yet. There has been no — there has been no woman I could have wanted near me at all in eleven years. Not one. Not even to talk to. Because I knew what would happen the first time I let myself want one." He looked at her. "I am letting myself want one now. I do not know that I will be able to stop wanting her. And the men with the SUVs are still in the country."

"I know," she said quietly. "I'm not afraid of them either."

He closed his eyes.

"You should be."

"Then I should be," she said. "I'm still here."

He cooked.

That was the strange grace of it — that after he had said the things he had said, the next thing he did was cook. He made venison stew in the cast-iron skillet and a flat loaf of brown bread on the woodstove, and he heated water in a kettle and brought her a tin cup of nettle tea he had collected in summer, dried, and put away in jars on a high shelf. She watched him work and slowly the cabin went warm around them, the woodstove ticking, the lantern soft.

They ate at the small table.

He ate, this time, with both sides of his mouth, because he was looking at her while he ate, and she had said tell me what you are, and the part of him that hid was, for the first time in a long time, not entirely on duty.

Afterward, he washed the dishes. She dried them. The small domestic motion of two pairs of hands at the same basin felt like a country she had never been to.

When the dishes were done, she said: "I'll sleep in the bed. You sleep on the floor by the stove. Same as last time."

"All right."

"Not because I'm afraid of you."

"I know."

"Because if I sleep in this bed with you tonight I will not be able to think clearly tomorrow. And I need to think clearly tomorrow. Because I am going to decide whether to stay here for the rest of the winter, and you are going to decide whether you want me to, and we have to decide it like people who slept apart."

He smiled then. A real smile. The first one. It cracked the lower line of his face and made him, for a moment, a young man.

"Andrea Ostrom," he said. "I want you to stay."

She had to look away from him to keep her own face.

"Sleep on the floor."

"Yes."

She did not sleep.

She lay in his bed in her clothes with the Glock under the pillow that she had stopped pretending was the reason for the Glock under the pillow, and she listened to him in the dark on the oilcloth between the bed and the woodstove, and she listened to her own pulse, and she listened to the wind in the lodgepoles, and she did not sleep.

After a long time, she heard him say, very quietly, from the floor:

"Andrea."

"Yes."

"Thank you. For naming her. For Maeve."

She closed her eyes.

"You're welcome, Kael."

He did not say anything else. She listened to his breathing slow.

Eventually, she did sleep. Not much, and not well. But she slept.

In the morning, she would tell him she was staying.

Chapter 7

Fire

She told him in the morning.

They were drinking coffee at the table. The window above the sink had frosted in a perfect lace overnight. The fire was just up, and the cabin was just warm, and the carving of the small wolverine sat between them where she had put it the night before.

She said: "I'm staying."

He set his cup down very slowly.

"For how long."

"Through the end of my field season. April."

He looked at her for a long moment.

"You'll need to tell your supervisor something."

"I'll tell him I've extended my grid because of activity I want to monitor closely. I'll tell him I'll radio in once a week from the high meadow. I'll tell him I've made an arrangement to stay closer to my data. Which is —" she gestured at him with the cup, in a small joke neither of them quite smiled at — "true."

"He'll come up."

"He won't. He has tenure track committee meetings until March. He'll be relieved I'm not asking him to. He's done four winters here. He prefers his fireplace."

"And in March?"

"In March I'll write him a report so good he won't ask any more questions for a year. Then I'll go down for my defense, and I'll come back."

The corner of his mouth.

"And me," he said.

"You decide."

He looked at the carving on the table. He turned it once with one finger. The little wolverine spun a half-turn, then settled.

"Andrea," he said.

"Yes."

"I want you to stay."

"Then I'll stay."

That was the whole of the conversation. He stood up and refilled both their cups. He sat back down. They drank their second coffee not looking at each other, in a silence that was not awkward and was not casual, but was, instead, the quiet of two people standing at the edge of a country and waiting for the light to come up.

The day went strange.

She had said I am going to think clearly today and instead all day she was — aware. Aware of him. Aware of where he was in the cabin, where his hands were, what he was doing with them. He spent the morning at the table sketching out a snare line on a scrap of paper. She spent the morning at her notebook making notes on the previous night's thermal images that she would never include in any report. When she got up to refill her cup, she walked behind his chair and her hand grazed the back of his neck for a half-second and they both went still for the same half-second, and neither of them said anything, and she went on to the stove.

She came back. She sat. She made another note. He went on with his snare line.

She had not been aware of a man's body in a room with her like this in two years. She had not been aware of one like this, with such fine and idiotic granularity, in maybe six.

She tried to work.

She watched him put away the snare-line paper after a while and go outside to feed the woodpile. The way the door closed behind him she felt in her ribs.

This is going to be a long winter, she thought.

She did not yet think: this is going to be a long night.

The wind came up in the afternoon.

Not a storm — a front. A real cold front, the kind that drops thirty degrees in three hours and presses down on the country like a shut door. By dusk the wind was screaming through the lodgepoles and the cabin walls were ticking. She helped him bring in the last of the kindling stack. They put a second blanket on the bed. They banked the woodstove for the long burn.

At nine the pipes started to thunk.

He went under the cabin with a kerosene lamp and a roll of rags and a heat strip he had wired together five winters back. She held the lamp and the wire ends while he worked.

"I'll have to keep them moving all night," he said when he came up. He had grease on his cheek. "I've got a heat tape that runs but it's not enough at this temperature. I'll trickle the taps and check them every hour."

"All night?"

"All night. If they freeze, we lose the inlet. I can't fix the inlet until thaw."

She thought.

"There's room enough for both of us in the bed," she said. "If you sleep on the floor by the stove, the cold is going to win every two hours. If you sleep in the bed, the cold doesn't get a chance."

He looked at her.

"Andrea —"

"Just to sleep, Kael."

He looked at her.

"Just to sleep?" he said.

"Just to sleep."

He looked at her for another long, slow second. He did not smile, and he did not look away.

"All right," he said. "Just to sleep."

She lay in the bed first. He banked the fire. He trickled the kitchen tap to a thin steady stream that just barely cleared the basin and ran into the drain. He laid out his boots by the door. He banked the fire again, more carefully. He stood at the bedside in the lantern light and looked down at her with his hands at his sides like a man at a threshold.

She held the corner of the blanket back.

He sat. He took off his boots and his suspenders and his flannel. He left his undershirt and his long underwear. He put a hand on the bed frame and then he lay down beside her.

The bed was a single. There was no version of him lying on it that did not put his shoulder against her shoulder.

His arm went along the headboard above her head. Her hip pressed against his hip. His ribcage rose and fell against the side of her ribcage. He smelled like cedar smoke and the working day and the faintest under-note of something colder and older.

He did not move.

She did not move.

A minute went by like that. Then two. The fire ticked. The wind outside cracked one of the high boughs in the lodgepole stand. The cabin breathed.

She turned her head a fraction and his head turned the same fraction and there was no further deciding to be done.

She kissed him.

He kissed her back. Slowly at first — careful, the way he had been careful the first time. Then less carefully. His mouth opened against hers and his hand came down off the headboard and slid behind her neck and held the back of her head, and she felt his other hand brace, slowly, on the bed frame beside her hip. He was holding himself off her. He was holding her toward him and holding himself off her at the same time.

She put her hand on his chest. Through the undershirt. She felt his heart hammering — faster than a man's heart should hammer; almost too fast to count. She felt the heat of him soak through the thin cotton into her palm. She felt, under her hand, the chord of muscle along his sternum.

She moved her hand down.

"Kael," she said. Her voice had gone low and rough.

"Yes."

"I don't want to sleep."

He put his forehead against hers. He breathed out, long.

"Andrea —"

"I'm not asking you for anything I haven't been asking you for since the porch." She moved her mouth against his again, light, asking. "You've been holding yourself off me for two weeks. Don't hold off me tonight."

His hand at the back of her neck tightened — gently, with that same tucked-away care — and his hips came down against hers and she could feel through her clothes and his that he had been holding himself off her in every way, all of those days.

She made a small sound. Not from pain. From the simple animal pleasure of being met.

He kissed her again. Harder this time. His other hand let go of the bed frame and found her waist and slid up under the thermal of her base layer to flat skin, and the cold of his palm — he was always a little colder than he should be, on the surface — made her gasp, and the gasp broke whatever the last of his restraint had been.

He undressed her slowly. He undressed her the way he did everything: with the economy of a man who had thought about every motion before he made it. He drew her thermal off over her head. He drew the long underwear down her legs. He sat back on his heels at the foot of the bed and looked at her — a long, slow look in the lantern light, his face open, his eyes black at the pupil and bark-gold at the iris, his hands resting on his thighs as if he did not yet trust himself to use them.

She watched him look at her.

"Kael."

"Yes."

"You too."

He undressed in two motions — the undershirt off over his head, the long underwear stripped down and kicked away. His body in the lantern light was the body she had glimpsed by the maul and the body she had seen kneeling naked in the snow, but here, close and warm and lit, it was different. The long parallel scars across his ribs. The flat plane of his stomach. A second set of scars at his hip — smaller, claw marks, but neat, almost decorative. The shape of him at the join of his hips, ready, more obviously than a man would have been because he was not entirely man-shaped at this temperature and at this attention, and she did not look away from any of it.

He came down to her.

The first time was not slow.

He had been alone for eleven years. She had been alone in a different way for two. They met somewhere in the middle and there was nothing of the bed that did not — within five minutes — feel inhabited. He was careful with his teeth. She felt him be careful — felt the corner of his mouth catch and pull and not — felt his breath go ragged at her throat where the pulse beat. She was not careful at all. Her nails went down his back in a way that, in the morning, would still be visible.

He kept saying her name. Quietly, against her ear, against her shoulder, against her hair. Andrea. As if naming her were a part of the act. As if he had needed to name her to do it.

She came twice. The second time he came with her, hard, with a sound that was not entirely human and that she would, later, remember as the moment she truly understood what she had said yes to.

He held himself over her after, propped on one forearm, looking at her face.

"Are you all right," he said. His voice was hoarse.

She laughed. The laugh broke at the end. She put her hand against his cheek.

"I'm all right."

"I didn't —"

"I know."

He lowered himself onto her — not heavy; he kept his weight on his forearms — and she pulled him down, pulled him in, until he gave in and was just on her, all of him along all of her, his heart hammering against hers.

They lay like that for a long time.

The wind outside had not stopped. The fire ticked. The taps trickled in the kitchen, steady. He had not gone to check them. He would, soon.

She traced her fingers along the parallel scars across his back.

"Tell me," she said quietly.

He breathed out. Slow.

"It was the night they took her," he said. "She had hidden me in a den at thirteen hundred meters. She told me to stay there until dawn. I broke her instructions. I came down. I saw what they were doing and I went after the one with the pistol." A pause. "He was bigger than me, in my form. He had a knife. I was lucky to leave there."

He stopped. He breathed.

"I have hidden the knife in my hip and the rest in my ribs ever since. The truth is I was not lucky to leave there. I should have stayed. I should have died with her. But she had told me to stay in the den, and she had told me to be small, and I had broken those instructions and I had still lived, and I have spent eleven years trying to make sense of that."

Andrea kissed his shoulder. The one with the scars.

"And tonight?" she said.

He was quiet for a long beat.

"Tonight is the first time I've stopped trying."

She pulled him closer. She did not have an answer to that.

He fell asleep on her shoulder after a while. She lay awake longer, looking at the small ring of light the lantern made on the cabin ceiling, listening to the cold thunk in the pipes and to the steady, slowing beat of his heart against her ribs.

Chapter 8

The Men in Black SUVs

The plane went over on the third afternoon.

They were on the porch — she'd been chopping kindling, he'd been planing a new haft for the broken maul — when the sound came up over the eastern ridge. Not a bush plane. A turboprop, single engine, the engine note unsteady in the way an engine sounds when a pilot is throttling low to listen.

Kael set the plane down on the porch rail.

The aircraft passed about a thousand feet above them, slow. It banked once over the clearing, the wing dipping. Then it climbed and continued west and the engine note returned to a steady cruise.

It came back twenty minutes later.

This time it crossed even slower, and lower. She could see, when she looked, the registration on the tail — small, the way ferry registrations get painted small — and the pale belly of the plane in the late winter light. It passed and did not return.

They stood on the porch a long time after the sound had gone.

"They're back," he said. Quietly.

She had not asked.

"How do you know."

"I have not heard a plane fly the watershed in two summers. They are good pilots. They fly that pattern when they are looking for ground sign and unsure where to look."

She put down the splitting maul. She put her cold hand inside his hand.

"How long do we have."

"A day. Maybe two. Whoever it is, if they had a precise fix, they wouldn't be flying. They'd be in. They're looking."

"What do we do."

He looked at her for a long moment. The bark of his eyes was, in this light, the color of a worn coin.

"We hide."

They spent the rest of the afternoon and all of the evening preparing.

He had a plan. He had had it for eleven years. There was a deep dry cellar under the cabin that he had reinforced over time — not just a root cellar, but a hide, with a second cached exit through a culvert that had once drained meltwater and now ran into a hollow under the deadfall fifty yards behind the cabin. He had stores there: rifle ammunition, jerky, dried fruit, a winter sleeping bag, a small kerosene heater. He showed her every inch of it. He showed her how the trap door opened from inside.

He showed her how to lock it from the inside.

He showed her the rifle. He walked her through it twice. He had her dry-fire it twice. She was already a competent shooter; he was a kind teacher who made small corrections in his careful low voice and seemed pleased when she got something right.

He packed a separate go-bag for himself. Not for the cellar — for deeper. A small pack with two pounds of jerky, a heavy wool blanket, his knife, a single tin cup.

"You can't carry the rifle as the other," she said.

"No."

"What do you do if they have rifles."

"I outrun them."

"They have a plane."

"Snow will be coming tonight. By tomorrow afternoon the plane won't be able to see ground."

"You hope."

"I hope."

He looked at her, and the bark eyes were soft in a way she had not yet seen in them. He had been crying, she realized. Not since the plane — at some earlier moment, when she had been outside chopping. He had been crying quietly while he planed a haft for a maul, and now he was not crying, but it was on his face.

"Andrea," he said.

"Don't."

"Andrea, if I have to lead them away —"

"Don't."

"If I have to lead them away, you stay in the cellar. You do not come out for two days. There is enough water and there is enough food. At the end of two days you go down to your base camp by the south trail and you do not come back here for any reason."

"I won't —"

"Andrea."

She closed her mouth. The lantern light moved on his face. He put his palm against her cheek. He had not done this before. The flat hand against the side of her face.

"This is what I have," he said. "Hiding you is what I have. Please let me have it."

She put her own hand over his.

"All right."

"You promise me."

"I promise."

He held her face for another long beat. Then he kissed her, lightly, on the forehead — a kiss she would think about, later, with a kind of grief that she did not know yet she would feel.

He stepped back.

"All right," he said. "Now. We have one more night and most of tomorrow before the storm. Come help me move the food."

They worked the rest of the evening. They moved the cellar stores in two trips. They packed her own pack with her notebooks, the receiver, two days of food, the Glock, two boxes of nine-millimeter. They left her sleeping bag in the cellar already laid out so she could fall straight into it. He walked the perimeter of the cabin once, in the dark, with his rifle. He did not find anything. He came back inside and put the rifle on its hooks and stood for a moment in the middle of the room as if listening.

The storm rolled in around midnight, exactly as he had said it would.

The wind came up. The snow began. Inside the cabin, the lantern flickered once and held.

He turned to her.

"We have tonight," he said. "Come to bed."

The second time was not like the first time.

The first time had been hunger. The first time had been eleven years and two years and a kerosene lantern and the cold thunking in the pipes. The first time he had been careful with his teeth in the way of a man not yet sure he could be careful at all.

This time he knew he could.

This time he moved slowly. He took her clothes off slowly. He kissed her at the throat, the collarbone, the place under her ear, the inside of her wrist. He laid her down across the wool blankets and looked at her in the lantern light with the long careful look of a man who is saying goodbye to a thing he might not see in this same shape again. He took his time.

She let him.

She had been ready to drive. She had been ready, in the cellar earlier when he had said if I have to lead them away, to take him to bed and dig her nails in and ride out whatever was coming with a kind of fury. She had not wanted to be tender. Tenderness, she had decided, was something you owed a future.

He gave her tenderness.

He gave her his mouth at her shoulder. He gave her his hand low on her belly. He gave her his weight in pieces, careful, his eyes black at the pupil and bark-gold at the iris and on hers the entire time. He came into her so slowly she could feel every inch of it as he gave it. He held himself there a long moment. He breathed her hair.

"Andrea," he said.

She closed her eyes.

"Yes."

He began to move.

She had thought, after the first time, that he had given her everything he had. He had not. He had given her hunger; now he gave her something else — a kind of grave, slow attention that took her apart not by friction but by patience. He watched her face. He watched the way her mouth opened. He watched the way her hands gripped his arms. He kept the pace careful and exact for a long time. When she finally went, she went with a small surprised cry against the side of his neck, and he held still inside her through it.

Then he went again, slower.

He brought her to it a second time.

The third time he started moving, she put her hand against his cheek and said: "With me."

He looked at her.

"With you."

He came with her. Quietly, this time. The bark-gold of his eyes did not go black. His face stayed his face. He breathed against her shoulder and his hand on her hip tightened and he came in a long shudder that she felt all the way down her spine.

Afterward, he lay along her side and did not move.

She put her face in his hair.

"Stay," she said. It was not, she would realize later, just about that night.

"I'll stay as long as I can."

"Promise me."

"I promise you."

She felt his mouth move at the side of her neck. Felt his teeth — gently, with that careful tucked-away precision — press against the skin where her neck met her shoulder. Not break. Just press. A long moment of pressure. The way an animal might claim a thing without taking it.

When he drew back, there was, on the skin, a mark. Two small ovals. Not bleeding. But unmistakable.

"Kael —"

"I know," he said quietly. "Forgive me. I needed — I needed to."

She put her hand over the place. She felt the heat of the mark under her palm. She felt the place his mouth had been. She did not understand what had happened, exactly. She understood that it was a kind of grammar he was speaking — something his species said, in pressure and not blood.

She lifted her hand. She put her nails — they were still painted, faintly, from a manicure she'd had three months ago in Missoula — against the inside of his shoulder.

She dug them in.

Slowly. Deliberately. She watched his face — the way his jaw worked, the way his breath caught.

She held them there until she had broken the skin in five small crescents.

She lifted her hand. There was a small ribbon of his blood on her thumb.

She put her thumb against her mouth and tasted it.

He watched her. His pupils widened. He made a small low sound in his throat.

"Andrea Ostrom," he said.

"Yes."

"You are not entirely human either."

She kissed him.

"No," she said. "Apparently I am not."

She slept then. Hard and dreamless. The wind outside roared and the cabin held and the woodstove ticked.

He did not sleep. She knew because at some point in the very early morning she woke briefly to find him propped on his elbow, looking down at her. His face in the lantern light was sad.

"Sleep," she said. She put her hand on his chest.

"I'm watching you sleep. It is enough."

"Lie down. Please."

He lay down. He put his arm under her head. She found his hand at her shoulder and held it.

She slept again.

In the morning the snow was so heavy you could not see the porch from the bed.

Chapter 9

The Hunt

The hunters came at noon.

The storm had broken by mid-morning, the way fronts break in the Bitterroot — abruptly, the sky going from low grey to washed blue in the space of an hour. Andrea had spent the morning at the table with the rifle across her knees, not because she expected anything but because handling the rifle had become a way to make the waiting bearable. Kael had been outside since first light. Checking sightlines. Brushing the new snow off the cabin's south side so it would not slide as a tell. Setting two small twined branches at the points where the perimeter trails approached the cabin — branches that would lie a certain way if a man walked through them, a different way if an animal did.

He came inside at eleven. He kissed her at the top of her head and went to the basin and washed his face. He sat at the table and ate jerky with both sides of his mouth.

At eleven-fifty he stood and went to the window and looked north.

He stood there a long time.

He turned to her.

"They're coming," he said quietly. "Two men. Snowmobiles a half mile out. They've left them at the meadow. They're on foot now."

She closed the rifle's bolt.

"Down," he said. "Now."

She went down the cellar ladder with the rifle and her pack. He stood at the trapdoor above her and looked down. He had his pack on already. His own rifle was slung across his back. His hair was loose, untied; he had taken it down at some point in the morning without telling her.

"Andrea."

She looked up.

His face in the trap of light from above was very still.

"Two days," he said. "Do not come up for two days, no matter what you hear."

"Kael —"

"Two days. Then the south trail. You do not come back to this cabin."

"Kael —"

He closed the trapdoor.

She heard the wooden bar drop on top of it. She heard him walk away across the floor. She heard the cabin door open and close, and his footfalls on the porch, and then his footfalls on the snow, lengthening, going faster — running — and then the rhythm of the footfalls changed and there were four of them, not two.

She listened to him run away from her in the body she had not entirely seen yet.

Then there was nothing but the cellar and the small ticking of the kerosene heater and her own breath.

The first rifle shot came at twelve-fourteen.

She knew because she had her watch on a strap across her wrist and she had been counting minutes to keep her mind in her body. The shot was distant — half a mile east, maybe more. It was a clean single crack, the way a hunting rifle cracks at distance.

The second shot came eighteen seconds after the first.

Then there were three quick shots in succession. Pop pop pop.

Then nothing for a minute.

Then a sound she would remember, later, as the moment she broke her promise. A sound she could not have predicted she would ever hear in her life. A sound that was not human and was not animal and was something else — the keening, broken-throated scream of an animal that had been hit hard and that knew it.

It went on for three seconds.

It stopped.

She came up the ladder.

She did not remember deciding. She did not remember unbarring the trapdoor. She did not remember reaching back for the rifle. She remembered the lid pushing up against her shoulders and the cold air rushing in and the cabin in midday light and her own boots on the porch and her own breath coming hard.

She listened for the direction. She had the high meadow east. The shots had come from beyond it, in the deep fir below the bowl. She started up the trail at a run that was almost an out-of-body thing — her legs, her lungs, the rifle barrel cold in her hand, the snow deep around her snowshoes which she had not stopped to put on and that she had to go back and put on because she could not run in deep snow without them and so she did, in three swift motions, and started again.

She moved fast.

She moved faster than she had ever moved in this country.

She found the place a quarter-mile past the meadow.

There was blood on the snow. There was a lot of blood on the snow.

There was a man face-down at the foot of a young fir. Not Kael. A man in a white snow camo coat, a rifle near his hand, a small dark hole at the base of his neck. Andrea would, later, not remember whether she had checked his pulse. She would remember only that she had stepped over him.

Twenty feet beyond him, in a hollow where a deadfall had punched a clearing in the snow, was the other man.

He was crouched, his rifle braced, his eyes wide. He was looking at something behind a low ridge of snow. He was breathing hard. He had a radio at his hip and he was shouting into it — short, clipped phrases. Andrea did not hear them. She raised her own rifle.

She had not killed a person.

She had not killed an animal larger than a grouse.

She raised her rifle and put the small bead of the front sight on the seam where the snow camo ended at the back of his collar and she pulled the trigger.

The rifle kicked. The man went forward into the snow. He did not move again.

She breathed once, in.

She breathed once, out.

She walked.

She walked toward the low ridge of snow behind which the second man had been looking. She walked toward what he had been looking at. She walked with the rifle at her shoulder. She did not think. She did not feel. The thinking and feeling were a country she had decided she would visit later.

She came around the low ridge.

Kael was on his side in the snow.

He was bleeding from his hip and from his side and from a deeper wound somewhere under his jaw. He was half-shifted — half, the way she had not yet seen him, with the body of a man at the chest and shoulders and the head and ribs of a wolverine, the mid-shift caught and held by injury. His left arm was a man's arm. His right side was the dark dense fur of the other. His eyes were the bark-color of his man's eyes. They were looking at her.

He tried to speak.

He could not. The throat was wrong.

She put the rifle down beside him in the snow.

She knelt.

She put her hand on the human side of his face. He closed his eyes against her palm.

"I'm here," she said. "I'm here. I'm going to take you home."

He shook his head against her palm — a small movement, a denial.

"Kael Voss," she said. "If you think I am leaving you here in this snow you do not understand who you have spent six weeks with."

He made a small sound. It might have been a laugh. It came from the wolverine throat. It hurt to hear it.

She put her arms under his shoulders and her arms under his knees, and she lifted.

She could not have lifted him. He weighed two hundred pounds as a man. As a half-shifted creature he weighed more. She was five-foot-six and a hundred and thirty pounds. She could not have lifted him. That was a true statement that was nevertheless not what happened. What happened was that her body decided it could lift him and her body did. She walked through deep snow with him in her arms for the quarter-mile back to the cabin. She did not remember the walk. She remembered the door, and the table, and laying him down on the bed.

She field-dressed his wounds the way her father had taught her to field-dress an elk.

She had not done it on a body that was still alive and still bleeding. She did it now. She used his medical kit from the cellar and her hands and her own thermal under-shirt cut into bandages. She packed the wound under his jaw — that was the worst — with a strip of gauze and pressure. She cleaned the long graze along his ribs. She found the bullet in his hip, which had gone clean through and exited at the back of his thigh, and she packed both holes and bound them tight.

He was still half-shifted. She did not know if she could field-dress the wolverine half. She wrapped what she could of it. The dark fur of his right side took the blood differently than skin did. It stayed under her hand longer.

He did not pass out. He stayed conscious. His eyes followed her work.

When she was done, she sat in the chair beside the bed and watched him.

After a long time, slowly — as slowly as a tide coming in — the right half of him shifted back. The fur receded into pale skin. The ribcage smoothed. The shape of his jaw reasserted itself, with a long red line under it where the wound was. The bandages she had put on the wolverine side became bandages on a man's side. She moved them as he shifted, carefully, so the pressure did not break.

When the shift was finished, he was a man.

A man very badly hurt.

A man who had stopped bleeding because she had stopped him bleeding.

He lifted his hand. He found her wrist. He held it.

"Andrea," he whispered. His voice was a torn whisper. "You came up."

"Yes."

"I told you not to."

"I know."

"I'm — I'm glad."

She put her face in her hands.

She let herself cry, finally, for thirty seconds. Then she stopped.

"Drink water," she said. She held the tin cup to his mouth. He drank. "I'm going to make you broth. Don't die while I make broth."

"I won't."

He did not.

She did not sleep that night.

She kept the fire up and she changed his bandages and she made broth and she made him drink it and she lay down beside him on the narrow bed in her clothes and listened to his breath go in and out. His breath rattled. The wound under his jaw was the worst. She had packed it but the packing was wet again at midnight and she packed it again, and again at three.

By dawn his breath had quieted.

By dawn he had a fever — a low one, manageable, the body's honest response to the work it was doing. His color was better than it should have been. Whatever he was, his body healed faster than a man's.

In the grey hour before sunrise he opened his eyes and looked at her.

"Andrea."

"I'm here."

"Lie down with me."

She lay down beside him. He was hot against her. She put her head into the hollow of his shoulder on the side that was not wounded.

"Kael."

"Yes."

"Don't go anywhere."

"I'm not going anywhere."

He moved his good hand to her hair. She felt his fingers there, slow, gentle. She felt the long slow rise and fall of his ribs against her cheek.

"You came up," he said again.

"Yes."

"I'm glad you came up."

He was quiet for a long time.

She thought he had gone to sleep.

Then he said, almost too quietly to hear: "Andrea. Let me have you. Like this. Now. While I can still tell which one of us I am."

She lifted her head. She looked at him.

He was watching her. His face — even with the long red line under his jaw and the pallor of blood loss — was tender, and certain, and unafraid.

She understood what he was asking.

It would not be like the storm night. It would not be like the night before. It would be a slow, careful, knowing thing between two people who had almost not had it again. It would be the thing they would have if they did not know there was a tomorrow.

She moved against him. Carefully — for his side, for his hip, for the wound at his jaw. She kissed his mouth gently. She did not undress him. She moved his clothes only as much as she needed to. She moved hers only as much as she needed to. She was the one above him, this time, with her hands on his chest, taking great care of him. She did not move fast. She set the pace at the long slow breath of him under her, and she stayed at that pace, and she watched his face the whole time. He watched hers. He did not close his eyes. His hand on her hip was steady. His other hand on her shoulder, the one with the small set of crescent marks she'd left there days ago, was warm.

She came with him. Quietly. With her forehead against his. His name in her mouth.

After, she lay against him and she felt his heart beat — slower now, the rattle gone — and she felt his hand slowly stop being a steady pressure on her hip and become a still hand on her hip, and she realized he had fallen asleep.

She watched the dawn come up through the small frosted window.

He was alive.

She had killed two men.

The carving of the small wolverine was on the bedside table, where she had set it the night they had moved the food. She reached out and picked it up. She held it in her closed hand against her chest.

The wild had stayed.

She had not yet decided whether to.

Chapter 10

Where the Wild Stays

The third man did not come.

That was the first miracle, and Andrea was rationally suspicious of it for three days before she allowed herself to begin to trust it. She kept Kael in the bed. She kept the rifle by the door. She walked the perimeter every dawn, looking for tracks, looking for ground sign of men, looking for the small things — broken twig, displaced moss — that her training had taught her were the honest signs of a person who did not want to be found. She found none.

On the second day she walked back to the place where it had happened.

The two men were still there. The snow had not begun to cover them in any meaningful way; the daytime sun had crusted everything to a hard pack, but the cold had held. She looked at them. She did not feel what she thought she would feel.

What she felt was — practical. A practical assessment of what she would do.

She dragged the first one — the one she had stepped over — into the thicket of deadfall on the far side of the clearing. He went heavily, slow. She dragged him until her arms ached. She covered him with the deadfall, and over the deadfall with the new snow.

She came back. She did the same with the second.

She took both of their rifles. She took both of their radios. She took both of their wallets. She read their identification by the cold light of late afternoon: federal agents, both, with credentials from an office that, when she Googled it later from Missoula in the spring, did not appear to exist. The names were probably not their names. The cards were polished, professional, but with a hollow weight to them, as if they were of a kind of credential that everyone in a certain office carried and that no member of the general public would think to verify.

She burned the credentials in his stove.

She put the rifles in the loft of his cabin, wrapped in oilcloth. She put the radios under the cellar — turned off, batteries out.

She walked the snowmobiles, one at a time, two miles east into the trees and shoved them off a low ledge into a creek where the spring melt would slowly take them.

She came back and made him broth.

She had killed two men.

She had not yet cried about it.

She did not, in the years to come, ever cry about it. The wolf you are when a man comes to take your wolf does not, in the end, get to cry about it.

His fever broke on the fourth night.

By the seventh day he could sit up. By the tenth he could walk to the porch. The wound under his jaw closed at a speed she had never seen on a human body. By the second week the long red line was a thin pale scar, and by the third week, in certain lights, it was almost invisible.

He shifted twice more during the recovery — small, partial shifts, the body trying to ease itself, the way some bodies tried to find rest by changing position. He would lie in the bed in his man's form and the side of him that was not wounded would, for a few minutes, blur into fur, dark and dense, and then resolve back into skin. He was, he said, mostly in control. He apologized for the times when he was not. He did not need to.

She nursed him. She fed him. She emptied his bedpan. She slept beside him every night. She did not let him be afraid of her seeing any of it. She had crossed, killing those two men, into a country where there was no embarrassment of the body left between them.

He was, in late January, well.

In February the snowpack was high enough that no one was coming up the watershed. The agency that did not exist did not come back.

In March she radioed in from the high meadow. Her supervisor scolded her for her absences and praised her data — she had, after all, been doing real work at the cabin, between everything else, and her field notebooks were thick with observations of Gulo gulo movement that would form the spine of a remarkable dissertation. He told her to come down at the end of April for her defense.

She told him she would.

She came inside to the cabin and told Kael.

He nodded.

"All right," he said. "And then?"

She had known the question was coming. She had been turning it in her head for two months — in the night, on the trail, at the table, in his bed. She had been waiting for it.

"My defense is the second week of May," she said. "After that I will have my PhD. I will be qualified to do any wildlife biology I want to do in the United States or anywhere else with reciprocal credentials. My next planned grant is for two summers in the Yukon. I would like to do it. I would like to do it from a base of operations that is — closer. To here. To you."

He waited.

"My options are: a position at the Forest Service in Hamilton, which would put me forty miles from this cabin and let me come up most weekends, but would mean my name is on a federal database somewhere with a forwarding address that — those — people might one day take an interest in. A research position at Missoula, the same problem, sixty miles. A small consulting practice based out of a cabin I would buy, somewhere in the Bitterroot or Selway — closer, less traceable, less money. I have already done the math. The third one works. I can make it work."

"You'd give up an academic position."

"I'd give up an academic position."

"For me."

"For here. The position is incidental. I will not give up the wolverines. I will not give up the work. I am going to spend the next twenty years on the Mustelidae of the Northern Rockies whether I am holding hands with a wolverine shifter while I do it or not. The question is whether I am holding hands with him."

The corner of his mouth.

"And the answer?"

"Is yes."

He looked at her. The bark-gold eyes were soft in the firelight.

"Andrea Ostrom."

"Yes."

"You are the most remarkable thing that has ever happened to me."

"I know," she said.

He laughed. Real and clean. The way he had laughed the morning of the second day, on the trail.

He came across the room and put his arms around her and held her against him for a long moment. She put her face against his throat. The scar there — almost invisible now — was warm under her mouth.

"Stay," she said.

"I will stay."

"Don't make me go down to Missoula in May without knowing you will be here when I come back in June."

"I will be here when you come back in June."

"And every June after."

"And every June after."

In late April, two days before she left for Missoula, the first thaw came.

The snow on the south-facing slope above the cabin began to soften. The creek below her base camp ran clear and loud for the first time since November. The lodgepoles started smelling — that particular wet pine smell that was the first true sign of spring in the Bitterroot.

She made a list. She packed her notebooks and her receiver and the two boxes of nine-millimeter she had not used. She left the rifle at the cabin. She left most of her gear at the cabin. She would be back in three weeks.

He walked her down on the last morning.

The trail was patchy snow and patchy ground, the spring breaking through. They walked slow. He had a small carved object in his pocket; she could see the shape of it against the fabric. He had been working on something for days at the table, his head bent over a piece of cedar, and had not let her see.

At the base of the trail where her truck had been parked since November under a tarp, he stopped.

He took out the carved thing.

It was small. A small four-legged animal, the legs short and powerful, the body long, the head almost too small for the body. A wolverine. The match of the one his mother had made him.

He put it in her hand.

"For you," he said. "For Missoula. For — wherever."

She closed her hand around it.

"Kael."

"Don't say anything. Go drive your truck. Come back to me when you can."

She kissed him.

He held her for a long minute. He kissed her on the forehead — the same kiss he had given her in the cabin the night of the storm. Then he stepped back, and she got into her truck, and she started it, and she drove away.

She watched him in the rearview mirror until the trees swallowed him.

She came back at the end of May.

She had her PhD. She had given a defense her committee called exceptional. She had been offered the Missoula research position; she had declined it. She had been offered the Yukon grant; she had accepted it for the following summer. She had been congratulated by people she had not seen in two years, embraced by her mother, asked by her ex-fiancé to dinner — she had declined. She had bought, with her savings and a small grant from a private foundation, a one-room cabin and twelve acres of land twenty miles from Kael's cabin, accessible only by a logging road that the county did not bother to plow in winter.

She drove back up the watershed on the last day of May with the wolverine carving in the cup-holder of her truck and her grandfather's wedding band on a chain around her neck. The grandfather's ring had been her mother's gift to her at the defense. To know what's good for you, her mother had said, and to be ready when it shows up. Her mother had been wrong about many things. She had not, it turned out, been wrong about this.

She hiked in to Kael's cabin from the trailhead in the late afternoon.

She had not told him the date.

She came around the bend in the trail above the clearing and stopped.

He was on the porch. He had heard her coming for the last hour, probably. He was leaning against the rail with a tin cup in his hand and his hair tied back, watching her.

When he saw her he set the cup down and came down the porch steps and crossed the clearing in long strides and met her at the trailhead.

He did not say anything. He took her pack off her shoulders. He took her face in both his hands. He looked at her.

"Welcome home, Andrea Ostrom," he said.

"Hello, Kael Voss."

He smiled, and it was a smile so wide and easy it was almost a foreign object on his face.

He kissed her.

That night, on the porch, she sat in a chair he had built her with her cup of coffee in her hand and watched the treeline.

The sun was up. The first true spring dawn. The grasses at the edge of the clearing were already beginning to green. The lodgepoles smelled of sap.

A wolverine came out of the treeline.

It walked slowly across the clearing. It came to the porch. It looked up at her with bark-gold eyes.

She reached down. Without thinking. The way you reach down for a thing you have known a long time.

The wolverine pressed the great heavy bone of its head against her open hand. She felt the warmth of him, and the rough density of his winter coat, and the weight of him leaning against her, and the small soft sound he made in his throat — the same sound he had made against the side of her neck in the tent, a long time ago.

He stayed there a long moment.

Then he stepped back. He looked at her once more with the wrong eyes for an animal face, and the right ones, and turned, and walked back across the clearing to the line of trees.

He disappeared into the treeline.

Andrea sat on the porch.

She knew he would come back.

She knew she would always come here.

The wild stays.
