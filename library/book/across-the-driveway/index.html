Across the Driveway

A Summer · Stuff So Sweet

Cassie Reeves is thirty-eight years old, sixteen years into a marriage that has, in the last three years, quietly stopped being a marriage. Her husband Brett — a Boston litigator — has not touched her in eleven months. Her twin sons are at sailing camp. When the worst heatwave in a decade hits Greenwich and the power goes out across the cul-de-sac for fifty-eight hours, she finds herself the only adult on Cherry Lane except David Avery, the architectural historian who lives across the driveway with his constantly-traveling cardiologist wife and their two young children. They have been neighbors for seven years. By the fourth night, in her pool at midnight, the small box each of them had been keeping closed for a decade comes off. A literary romance about two grown people who fall in love in the wrong order, and the bittersweet adult choice they make six weeks later.

Chapter 1

The Heatwave

The power goes out at twelve past two.

I am, when it goes out, in the kitchen of my house in a tank top and an old pair of cutoff denim shorts and my hair piled up on top of my head with a chopstick I have not, in three years, used for any other purpose. I am making myself a glass of iced coffee that I do not, in any honest reckoning, need. It is ninety-four degrees outside. It is, I would have to admit if pressed, ninety-one degrees in the kitchen. The kitchen has central air. The central air has, since June, been working at the upper edge of what was promised at the install.

The fan above the stove dies first. Then the lights. Then the small whirring of the fridge.

I stand in the small silence of the kitchen with the iced-coffee glass in my hand and look at the ceiling and say, with the tired clean clarity of a woman who has spent forty-eight hours managing twin sixteen-year-olds and a husband leaving for Aspen at five in the morning, "Of course you have."

I check my phone.

Eversource has, in the small bored corporate way of a utility company, sent me a text. We are aware of an outage in your area. Estimated restoration: 9pm tonight.

The estimated restoration is, of course, a lie.

I know, in some small interior part of myself, that it is a lie because — in the seven years I have lived on this cul-de-sac — outages on Cherry Lane have never been restored before forty-eight hours. There is something about the way the grid feeds our six houses. It has been, in our previous outages, a small and increasingly tense joke between Bob Marland next door and my husband Brett, who is, today, not on Cherry Lane but in Aspen.

I check the temperature again.

Ninety-five outside. Ninety-two in.

I take a long sip of the iced coffee.

I think, with the small clean clarity I have learned to apply, in the last twenty months, to the small failures of my domestic life: all right. Phase one.

Phase one is: get the chicken out of the freezer and into a cooler with ice. Phase one is: get the milk into the cooler too. Phase one is: open all the windows in the house, downstairs first, then upstairs, in the proper rotation. Phase one is: turn off the central air at the breaker so it does not, when the power comes back, surge and trip. Phase one is, then, when phase one is done, to go out to the pool and float in it, in the only floatable in the garage, for an hour, while I do not text Brett about it.

I do all of these things.

The pool is the one good thing about this part of phase one. The pool is — I should be clear — Brett's pool. He had it dug in 2021 with a small inheritance from his uncle. He told me at the time it was for the boys. It is, in any honest reckoning, also for me. He swims in it three times a year. I swim in it three times a week.

I am in the pool, on the long pink rubber flamingo with the half-deflated head, in the second hour, when I hear the gate at the far end of the property open.

I lift my head.

It is, across the lawn, David Avery.

He is, when I see him, in a white linen shirt rolled to his elbows and a pair of grey cotton shorts. He has, with him, his daughter Iris — who is, this summer, seven — in a small navy swimsuit with a sunhat on her head, and his son Sam — who is ten, and who has the gangly knees of a ten-year-old boy who has had a big summer. They are both carrying small towels. Iris is holding a small purple watering can.

He stops at the gate.

He sees me on the flamingo.

He has, I notice, the small careful pause of a man who has not, in their seven years of being neighbours, ever once come into my yard without a previous text.

"Cassie."

I sit up on the flamingo.

This is, by the way, a more difficult manoeuvre than I would have, twenty seconds ago, given it credit for.

"David."

"Are we — I am very sorry. The power is out at our house. The kids are very hot. I — Mira is in Singapore, and I — would you mind, terribly, if Iris and Sam used your pool for an hour. I will, of course, sit with them. I won't —"

He stops.

He is — and I notice this with the small editorial clarity of a woman who has, in seven years, watched him do many small managed things across the driveway — flustered. David Avery is not, in my limited experience of him, a flustered man. He is, in any given small interaction at the mailbox, a man with the careful settled posture of a man who has decided to be unflappable about everything his life is going to throw at him.

He is, today, flustered.

I am, today, in the pool on a pink flamingo in a green-bikini-top and cutoff denim shorts that, when wet, have stopped being shorts and started being something closer to a small denim idea.

I get off the flamingo.

I do this with as much grace as one can get off a flamingo.

I walk across the pool to the steps.

I climb out.

He is, when I climb out, looking very specifically at a point on the lawn three feet to my left.

"David."

"Cassie."

"Of course they can use the pool. Of course. Hello, Iris. Hello, Sam."

Iris, who is seven, waves vigorously. Sam, who is ten, says — with the careful courtesy of a boy who has been raised by Mira Avery, world-class cardiologist who travels three weeks of every month — "Hi, Mrs. Reeves. Thank you so much."

"You are very welcome, Sam."

I do not, while I am saying this, look at David.

I do not look at David because, at the moment I climbed out of the pool with the cutoff shorts plastered to my hips and the green bikini top — which has, in the time I was on the flamingo, gone slightly transparent — I caught, in my peripheral vision, the small careful interior gathering-of-self of a man who had been about to look at me directly and had, in the half-second, decided very firmly not to.

I have not, in eleven months, been not-looked-at in that particular way.

I have, in eleven months, not been looked at at all.

The small interior of the not-looking is, I notice, more attention than I have had from any man in our seven-year tenure on Cherry Lane.

I wrap a towel around my waist.

I gesture at the pool.

"Come in. You have it for the afternoon. There are sodas in the cooler in the garage — the cooler with the blue lid — and I have, in the small fridge in the cabana, two unopened boxes of those popsicles Iris likes. The watermelon ones."

Iris's face — which had been holding the small composed sad face of a seven-year-old in a power outage — undid itself into the entire small unedited joy of a child who has just learned about watermelon popsicles.

"Cassie."

I look at David.

He looks at me.

He has, this time, looked at me. Not at the bikini, not at the cutoffs, not at any small part of me. At my face.

"You don't have to —"

"David."

"Yes."

"It is the middle of a heatwave. Your children are hot. My boys are at sailing camp until Sunday. Brett is in Aspen. I have, in this house, by myself, a pool, two boxes of popsicles, an inflatable flamingo, and approximately ninety-six unbroken minutes before sundown that I do not need anyone for. Take the pool. Take the popsicles. Take the cabana. I am going to sit on the patio under the umbrella and read a book. I am offering this to you with no strings attached and no calculation involved and I am going to be, in the next sixty seconds, indignant if you turn me down."

He looks at me.

His face, when I have said all of that, has been doing — I noticed this clearly — a small careful thing. It has been deciding, behind the small unflustered surface, whether the offer is, in fact, what it sounds like. He has been weighing the seven years of Cherry Lane and the seven years of seventeen real conversations and the small interior fact, which I knew without his telling me, that he had not, in those seven years, ever once been in my pool. He has been weighing the small specific information — gathered, I would later understand, from years of mailbox encounters — that I am Cassie Reeves, of Brett-and-Cassie-Reeves, who throw a small Christmas party every December at which he and his wife stand politely with a glass of wine for an hour and then leave.

He has, in the small fluster of his arrival, been telling himself the offer cannot be what it sounds like.

He sees, in my face, that it is.

"All right," he says. "Thank you, Cassie. We will not stay long."

"Stay as long as you want."

They stay until eight-thirty.

I do, in the first hour, sit on the patio under the umbrella with my book. The book is one I have been meaning to read for two months. I do not, in the first hour, read any of it.

I watch — which I will, later, admit was the truth of the hour — David Avery sit at the edge of the pool in a white linen shirt with his rolled sleeves dipping into the water as he sat at the lip with his bare feet in, watching his two children do every cannonball and splash-game that children do in a pool when their mother is in Singapore and their father has not, in their living memory, used a real pool with them on a real Wednesday afternoon. He laughed at Iris twice. He helped Sam adjust his goggles. He kept, in the small careful way of a parent at someone else's pool, his eye on the deep end at every moment.

He did not look at me on the patio.

He did not look, especially, at the green bikini top, which I had — I will be honest about this — taken off in the cabana and replaced with a soft grey t-shirt before coming out.

He did not, in any of the seven years of Cherry Lane, see me as carefully as he was, in the hour, not seeing me.

At five, I went into the kitchen — the dark hot kitchen — and made a pitcher of lemonade by torchlight with the lemons that had been on the counter. I brought it out on a tray with five glasses and a stack of paper napkins. I set the tray on the patio table.

I poured a glass for Iris, and a glass for Sam, and a glass for David, and a glass for myself.

We drank the lemonade.

We sat at the patio table — Iris and Sam in their towels, eating their watermelon popsicles, drumming their wet heels against the chair legs — and we talked, the four of us, about the heatwave, and about sailing camp, and about Sam's school, and about Iris's reading list, and about the small Cherry Lane neighborhood gossip that David, who had been the primary parent for the last four years, knew with the small clinical detachment of a man who heard most things from school dropoff lines.

It was, I realized somewhere in the middle of it, the longest conversation David Avery and I had ever had.

He looked, in that conversation, at my face every time he spoke to me.

He spoke to me, in that conversation, the way one speaks to a person.

Brett had not, in eleven months, looked at me that way at our own kitchen table.

I drank the lemonade.

I tried not to think about it.

At eight, the sun went down. The temperature on the patio came, finally, down to something like merciful. I lit, on the patio table, three citronella candles. We sat under the small flickering of them with the second pitcher of lemonade. Iris fell asleep on her father's lap with a wet hand against the small pulse at his throat. Sam, ten, said in the soft small voice of a tired ten-year-old: "Dad. Are we staying for dinner."

David looked at me across the table.

"I have," I said, "a freezer full of chicken that is, by now, definitely defrosted. I have a grill that runs on a gas tank that has nothing to do with the grid. I have, in the pantry, an unopened bag of those soft rolls Iris likes. I have, on the side of the fridge, a sleeve of those popsicles you haven't had yet. If you would like to stay, you should stay. If you would like to go home and sit in your dark hot house in the second night of a heatwave with two tired children, you are also welcome to do that."

He looked at me.

He held my eye for a long beat.

"We will stay," he said. "Thank you."

We stayed.

I grilled the chicken on the patio while David put Iris in dry clothes from her bag and got Sam to wash his hands at the outdoor tap. We ate by candlelight at the patio table. The chicken was good. The rolls were good. Iris fell asleep again — for real this time, hard, the way seven-year-olds sleep when they have been swimming for four hours — at her father's elbow at the end of the meal.

David picked her up.

He stood at the edge of the patio with his daughter against his shoulder in the dark.

He said: "Cassie."

"David."

"This has been —" He stopped. He looked at me across the candlelight. "Thank you."

"You're welcome."

"I would invite you to come over, if I had anything other than a dark hot house to invite you to."

"I know."

"Cassie."

"Yes."

"Tomorrow."

"Tomorrow what."

"Tomorrow, when the power is still out, I am going to come back across the driveway in the afternoon. Sam will be at his friend Jacob's house from noon. Iris will be at her friend Lila's. I will, by three, be on Cherry Lane alone. I am asking you, tonight, whether — when I come back, in the afternoon — you would like company."

I looked at him.

I looked at his daughter asleep on his shoulder in the dark.

I looked at the small careful flicker of the citronella candles between us.

I said: "Yes, David."

"All right."

"Three o'clock."

"Three o'clock."

He carried Iris home across the gravel driveway in the dark, with Sam trailing behind him with the sleepy beach bag. The lights at his house were off. The lights at every house on Cherry Lane were off. The only light at all, from any direction, was the citronella candles on my patio, and the moon, and the small interior light inside me that, sometime in the last six hours, had unexpectedly come on.

I sat at the patio table for a long time after they had gone.

I drank the second pitcher of lemonade.

I told myself, in the small careful voice of a woman who had, that afternoon, decided to grill chicken for a man and his two children at an empty house in a heatwave: Cassie. You are forty-eight hours from this becoming a thing you cannot put back in the box.

I told myself: Cassie. You should not let it become a thing.

I told myself: Cassie. You have not been wanted in eleven months.

I told myself: Cassie. You will not, in any sane decision-making, let David Avery come back across the driveway tomorrow afternoon at three o'clock.

I sat at the patio table.

I did not — I will tell you now, so we can be honest with each other from the start — believe any of the things I had told myself.

I went to bed at eleven. I lay in the dark hot bedroom in a thin nightgown with the windows open and the small noise of the cul-de-sac at night around me, and I thought about a man in a white linen shirt with the sleeves rolled to his elbows sitting at the lip of my pool with his bare feet in the water and his hand at the small of his daughter's back, and I thought about him looking at my face across the candlelight.

I did not, that night, sleep very much.

I was not, when I did sleep, dreaming about my husband.

Chapter 2

The Patio

He comes at three on the dot.

I have, by then, been moving around the kitchen of my house in a thin white sundress for forty minutes. I have not eaten lunch. I have, twice, gone into the bathroom upstairs and looked at myself in the mirror in the way I have not, in three years, looked at myself in the mirror. I have, on the second of those times, taken my hair down from the chignon I had been wearing for fifteen years of my life and re-piled it on top of my head in a soft messy bun, and I have, in the small interior way of a woman who is making decisions she does not, yet, admit she is making, dabbed a small amount of perfume — Diptyque Philosykos, the bottle Brett gave me for my thirtieth birthday — at the inside of each wrist.

I tell myself, in the kitchen at two fifty-five, that this is, in fact, simply hospitality. That this is, in fact, simply being the kind of neighbour I have always wanted to be. That this is, in fact, a small Wednesday afternoon during a heatwave when the only adult on the cul-de-sac who is not me has two children to manage, and that the kind thing — the normal thing — is to be civil.

I tell myself this in the kitchen at two fifty-five.

At three I hear the gate.

I am, when I hear it, at the sink with my hand on the tap. I have, the second before, opened the tap to fill a pitcher. The tap does not produce water because the pump runs on the power that is, on day three of the heatwave, still out. I have, in the small mechanical embarrassment of a woman who has lived in this house for seven years and has never quite remembered which things are gravity-fed and which are not, stood at the sink with my hand on the dry tap for an entire half a second.

I turn around.

He is in the door of the kitchen.

He has, this time, knocked at the screen door instead of the kitchen door. I had not heard the knock because I had heard, against the small Wednesday silence of a cul-de-sac with the grid down, the gate first.

He says: "Hi."

He is in a black linen shirt this time. Untucked. The sleeves not rolled. A pair of dark jeans. Loafers without socks. He is — and I notice this, because I am, at this point in my afternoon, a woman who has spent forty minutes thinking about this — clean-shaven. He smells faintly of cedar.

I say: "Hi."

He says: "I brought something."

He has, in his hand, a small bottle of white wine in a small cooler bag with a single ice pack. He has, with his other hand, a brown paper bag from the artisan bakery on Greenwich Avenue — the one with the small ribbon-tied baguette in it that I can see from the angle of the bag.

"David."

"Yes."

"You brought a bottle of wine and a baguette to my house at three on a Wednesday in a heatwave."

"I did."

I look at him.

He looks at me.

For a long beat, neither of us speaks.

He sets the wine and the bag on the kitchen counter.

He says: "Cassie."

I say: "David."

He says: "I am, right now, asking you whether what I think this is, is in fact what you also think this is. Because if it is not, I will pour us a glass of wine and we will eat the baguette and I will go back across the driveway at five o'clock and tomorrow morning we will, when we pass at the mailbox, be neighbours of the kind that we have been for seven years. And if it is, I will pour us a glass of wine and we will, very probably, not eat the baguette, and I will spend the rest of my life trying to be careful about what comes after."

I look at him.

I look at his face — which is, in the dim hot kitchen, the face of a man who has, in the twenty-four hours since the patio, decided that he is going to be honest about it.

I think: Cassie. He has given you a way out.

I think: Cassie. He has, by giving you a way out, said the thing it would have been impossible for you to say.

I think: Cassie. Take the way out.

I cross the kitchen to him.

I do not, when I get to him, kiss him. I do not, when I get to him, touch him. I stop a foot from him at the counter and I look up at him — he is, I notice now, six-two — and I say, in a voice I have not used in some time:

"It is what you think it is."

He closes his eyes.

He says, very quietly: "Cassie."

"Yes."

"All right."

"Pour the wine."

He pours the wine.

He pours the wine into two of the small good wine glasses I keep in the cupboard above the stove — the ones I have not, in the four years since I stopped having dinner parties, taken down for any purpose. He hands me mine. He does not, in the handing, touch my hand. We are both, in this part of the afternoon, being careful.

We take the wine outside.

We sit on the patio under the umbrella. The patio is in the shade, because it is three in the afternoon, and because the sun is at this hour west of the house. The temperature, even in the shade, is ninety-two. We do not, by mutual quiet agreement, comment on the heat.

We drink the wine.

We talk.

We talk for an hour about nothing. He tells me about his thesis advisor, a Dutch architectural historian who in the 1990s had taught him to look at every American mid-century lap pool as a small architectural confession. He tells me about a piece he is writing about the history of the carport. He tells me, in the small careful way of a man telling a woman about himself for the first time, about his mother, who is seventy-six, and who lives in Rhode Island, and who has, in the last year, started forgetting things. I tell him about my mother, who runs a small dance studio in Brooklyn, and who has, in the last year, started reading the New York Review of Books. I tell him, in the small careful way of a woman telling a man about herself for the first time, about a thing I have not told anyone except my college roommate Phoebe — that I had, when I was twenty-six, before the twins, briefly written a small column for Dance Magazine, and that I had stopped because Brett's career had started and there had not been time.

He listens.

He listens the way Brett does not, in any of our seven years, listen.

He listens with the small careful attention of a man who is, in some real way, hearing me.

I am, by the end of the hour, in a kind of small terror.

He looks at me across the patio.

He says: "Cassie."

"Yes."

"Are you afraid."

"Yes."

"Yes, I am."

"Of what."

"Of what we are about to do."

He looks at me.

He sets down his glass.

He says: "Cassie. We do not have to do anything tonight. We do not, in fact, have to do anything ever. I came over today because I had decided, last night, that I was, if you wanted to, going to do whatever you wanted to do. I am, now, sitting on your patio at the end of a glass of wine that has taken us an hour to drink, and I am, somehow, in a slightly different country. I am — I am no longer here only because I had decided. I am here because, sitting at this table looking at you, I have started to understand that I have, in some way, been looking at you across the driveway for seven years and have been, at the same time, telling myself I haven't. And I am terrified, Cassie. I am terrified because if we do, tonight, what we have, sitting on this patio for the last hour, been about to do, neither of us is the same person at eleven o'clock that we were at three. And I am very aware of who I am and who you are and what house I have to walk back to, and what kind of life I have decided to spend the rest of mine in. I am very, very aware. So I want to ask you, very carefully, what you would like to do tonight."

I look at him.

I look at this man across the patio table in the dark wood and the candle wax and the small honest hour we have just spent.

I think — for the first time in three years, with a small clear clarity at the centre of my chest — I think: I am going to do this. I am going to know what it is to be wanted again. I am going to know what it is to be looked at. I am going to know what it is to be in the room. I am going to know what it is to be Cassie. Once. Just for a while. And then I will be Cassie Reeves of Brett-and-Cassie-Reeves again and I will close the box and I will go on. But I am going to do this. I am going to.

I do not say any of that.

What I say is:

"Walk me out to the pool. Don't touch me. Not tonight. Tonight I just want to swim with you in the pool in the dark."

He looks at me.

He looks at me with the small private gratitude of a man who has, in being asked, been let off a difficult hook.

He says: "All right."

"Don't kiss me."

"I won't."

"Not tonight."

"Not tonight."

We walk down to the pool.

The pool is the only place on the cul-de-sac that is, by ten at night, cool — the water is the temperature of a tepid bath, after three days of ninety-six-degree air. The deck around it is still warm from the day. The cul-de-sac is dark. There are no streetlights — there are, for that matter, no lights anywhere — because the grid is, on Cherry Lane, still down. The only light is the moon, which is a thin near-new moon, low on the eastern horizon over the maple at the back of my property.

I undress on the deck.

I do not, in undressing on the deck, perform for him. I undress with my back to him in the dark. I do not, in undressing, take everything off. I leave on the sport bra and the cotton underwear I had been wearing under the white sundress, because I had, in the morning, made the careful decision not to come down to the pool in lingerie. I am not, even at the moment of doing this, choosing dishonesty. I am, simply, in undressing in front of David Avery for the first time, being a forty-eight-hour-old version of myself who is, at this stage, not ready to be fully unclothed in front of him.

He undresses too. He undresses without ceremony, behind me, in the dark — I hear, by sound, the linen shirt come off, then the jeans, then the loafers — and he steps to the lip of the pool in his boxer briefs and he goes into the water in a long quiet dive.

I follow him.

We swim, in the dark, for a long time. We do not, in the water, touch. We swim in long slow laps with the moon as the only thing in the sky, and the cul-de-sac asleep in the dark, and the gravel of the driveway visible — only just — between the two dark houses.

After a while, he stops at the deep end.

He hangs on the lip of the pool with his elbows hooked over the rim.

He looks at me from across the water.

I swim, slowly, to the deep end.

I stop at the lip beside him. I hang on the lip too. Our elbows are six inches apart on the white tile.

He does not look at me directly. He looks across the lawn at the dark of my house.

"Cassie."

"Yes."

"Tomorrow. The day after that. The next eight days. Until the children come back from camp on Sunday and your sons come back, and Mira comes back on Tuesday."

"Yes."

"You will think, tomorrow morning, about what we are doing."

"I will."

"You will think very hard."

"I will."

"If, when you have thought about it, you want me to come back at three o'clock again, you should text me at noon."

"All right."

"If you do not, I won't come."

"I know."

"Cassie."

"Yes."

"Tomorrow night you will be the one to decide."

"Yes."

I do not say, at the deep end of the pool in the dark with my elbows on the tile six inches from his: I have already decided.

I do not need to.

He, looking very carefully at the dark of my house and not at me, knows that I have.

He says, very quietly, looking at the dark: "All right."

We swim, for a while longer, in the dark.

He gets out at midnight.

He dresses on the deck with his back to me.

I get out and dress with my back to him.

He walks me to the kitchen door, in the dark. We do not — we do not touch. He stops at the door.

He says: "Goodnight, Cassie."

I say: "Goodnight, David."

He goes across the driveway.

I close the door.

I stand in the dark hot kitchen with the small interior fact that I have, this evening, decided.

I climb the stairs.

I lie down on the bed.

I do not sleep for two hours.

In the second hour, I roll onto my side and put my hand, slowly, between my legs, and I think — for the first time in three years — about a man's hand at the small of my back, and a man's mouth at the inside of my thigh, and I am, when I come into the small private quiet of my own bedroom, not thinking about my husband.

I am thinking about the white linen shirt with the sleeves rolled to the elbows, and the small careful flicker of citronella candles, and the long quiet swim in the dark.

I sleep, finally, at three.

In the morning, at noon, I text him.

I text him: Three.

He texts back, within thirty seconds: Three.

Chapter 3

The Pool at Midnight

He does not, on the second day, come at three.

He texts me at two forty-five.

He texts: I am sorry. Iris fell off her bike at Lila's. She is fine — small scrape on the knee. Lila's mother has just called and asked me to come pick her up. I will not, today, be able to come at three.

I read the text in my kitchen.

I — and I will say this in the small careful clarity of a woman who has, by this point, been waiting for an hour for the first sound of the gate — feel, in the first second, an entire physical disappointment in my body that I had not, before that second, prepared myself for.

I make myself stop.

I text back: Take care of her. Of course. I'm sorry.

He texts: Tonight. After they are in bed. I will text you when they have gone down.

I look at the text.

I think: Cassie. He is asking you whether tonight is, still, on.

I think: Cassie. This is the moment in which you can, with grace, say no. You can text back that you have, in the seven hours since you sent him three at noon, reconsidered. You can text back that you would prefer to not do this. You can text back that you would prefer, on reflection, to be neighbours of the kind you have been for seven years.

I think this for a long minute.

I text back: Tonight.

He texts back: Tonight.

I put the phone down on the counter.

Chapter 10

Across the Driveway

Six months go by.

That is the first thing to say about the rest of the book — that six months go by. The fall arrives on Cherry Lane. Brett comes home from Chicago. Mira comes back from her conferences. The boys go back to school.
