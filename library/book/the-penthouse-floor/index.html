The Penthouse Floor

A Manhattan Romance · Stuff So Sweet

Hadley Wright is house-sitting her best friend's family Tribeca apartment for six months — recovering from the discovery that her boyfriend of two years had a wife in San Francisco — when she meets Damon Kade in the elevator on her third night. He lives in the penthouse on the thirtieth floor. She lives on the fourteenth. He gets off the elevator on her floor to know her name before she disappears into 14C for the rest of his life. The next morning a flat white from her favorite café shows up at her door with a small handwritten card: If you don't want me to do this again, leave the cup in the lobby and I won't. She doesn't leave the cup in the lobby.

Chapter 1

Floor 14

I am, when I meet him, in the elevator with a Pomeranian under my arm and a paper bag of soup dumplings, wearing my old Parsons sweatshirt and the leggings I will not be photographed in.

The Pomeranian is Pretzel. The soup dumplings are the only good thing about my Thursday. The sweatshirt is the only thing I own that does not, in this building, look out of place — because in this building everything except the elevator is in conscious good taste, and the only people in unironic sweatshirts are people who own the building.

I get on at the lobby. The doorman — Marcus, who has decided in the three days I have lived here that I am a person worth liking — tips two fingers off his hat at me as the doors close. I nod back. Pretzel sniffs the corner of the soup-dumpling bag with a kind of polite middle-class outrage.

The elevator goes up. It stops at the fifth.

A man gets on.

I do not, when he gets on, look at him directly. That is the elevator etiquette of a building like this — you are aware of who is in the elevator without registering them as a specific human being. I clock, peripherally: tall, dark hair, a charcoal coat that costs more than my rent did when I had rent. A briefcase that has been through more boardrooms than I have. A faint citrus thing in the air around him — cologne or aftershave or the kind of soap men in this tax bracket wash their hair with at the gym.

He presses 30.

The elevator goes up.

We pass six.

We pass seven.

He says, in a low pleasant voice that has no business being directed at me on a Thursday at 8:47 in the evening: "Soup dumplings?"

I look at him for the first time.

It is the moment in romance novels at which the narrator notes some specific feature — the eyes, the mouth, the height. I will tell you what I notice, in the precise order in which I notice it.

I notice that he is looking at me.

Not at Pretzel. Not at the bag. Not at the floor above the door. At me. With the kind of unhurried, specific attention that men of my acquaintance reserve, in elevators, for their phones.

I notice that he is, in a way I have not encountered in nine months, beautiful. Not in the polished editorial-headshot way of the men I work with. In a tired, settled, slightly-rumpled way. As if he has been pleasant-looking his whole life and has, in some recent stretch of months, stopped sleeping enough to look perfectly pleasant, and the not-quite-pleasantness has, in some interior way, made him better-looking. Dark hair that has gone, at the temples, a quarter-shade lighter than the rest. A jaw that has been broken once and reset cleanly. Eyes the colour of slate at four in the afternoon.

I notice that I am, on Pretzel's behalf, suddenly conscious of my own breath.

"Joe's Shanghai," I say. "On Pell."

"You walked from Pell."

"I took the F."

He smiles. The smile is half a smile. The half he gives, I will later understand, is a half he gives everyone — the social half. The other half is held in reserve.

"Joe's Shanghai's better than they have any right to be."

"They are."

We pass twelve.

He says: "Your dog is staring at me."

"That's his thing."

"He's deciding whether to like me."

"He is. He has been told he is, at this stage of his life, allowed to dislike up to three people per quarter. He is conserving his disapproval."

The half-smile becomes a quarter more.

"What's the verdict, Mr. — ?"

"Pretzel," I say.

"Pretzel."

"Yes."

"Hello, Pretzel."

Pretzel, who is — and I want to be clear about this — a five-pound dog who has previously distrusted men, considers him gravely from the crook of my arm. He puts his small head sideways. He sniffs in the man's direction once. He returns to the soup-dumpling bag.

"He likes you," I say.

"I'm honoured."

We pass fourteen.

The elevator stops at fourteen.

The doors open onto the long quiet hallway of my floor — the polished marble, the brass sconces, the runner that has been replaced twice since I was in seventh grade — and I shift the soup bag to my other arm and step out.

He steps out with me.

I stop.

I turn.

He is standing in the elevator door, holding the door open with the flat of his hand against the rubber, watching me with an expression that is, by this point in the encounter, not the social half-smile and not even the quarter more. It is an expression I will spend most of the next six weeks trying to find an analogue for in any of the other men I have known. I will not find one.

"I'm sorry," he says. "Wrong floor."

"You pressed thirty."

"I know."

I look at him.

He looks at me.

For a long beat — longer than is etiquette-comfortable in any setting on the East Coast — neither of us moves.

He breaks first. He lifts his hand off the door. He extends it.

"Damon Kade," he says. "I live on thirty. I have lived on thirty for four years. I have not, in any of those four years, gotten off this elevator anywhere but the lobby and thirty. I am getting off here because I would like to know your name before you go into" — he glances briefly down the hall and back — "I'm guessing 14C, since the lights have been on at 14C for the past three nights and the lights have been off at 14C for as long as I've lived here. And I would prefer not to spend the next six months riding the elevator with a woman whose name I don't know."

I am, when I tell this story later, going to lie about what I said next. I am going to say I told him to get back in the elevator. I am going to say I told him I was busy. I am going to say I told him I was not, currently, available to be hit on in the hallway by men I had known for nine floors.

What I say is: "Hadley Wright."

He shakes my hand.

His hand is large and dry and warm and is gone again in less than a second.

"Hadley Wright. Sleep well. Good night, Pretzel."

He gets back into the elevator. The doors close.

I stand in the hallway for a long moment with Pretzel under my arm and the soup bag warm at my hip and my heart, my whole heart, doing the thing I have not let it do in nine months and twenty-two days.

I walk down to 14C.

I let myself in.

I put the soup dumplings on the counter.

I unclip Pretzel's lead. He runs across the parquet to his water bowl and drinks with a great affronted slurping. He has had, his body language tells me, an evening.

I have had, my body language tells my body, an evening.

I take off the Parsons sweatshirt. I stand for a long moment in the bathroom mirror in my old grey t-shirt and the leggings I will not be photographed in. I look at the woman in the mirror.

I tell her: No.

I tell her: Hadley. You just got out. Last man you trusted had a wife in San Francisco. You are not riding the elevator down the hill of a man you have known for nine floors of a Tribeca pre-war just because he has the kind of face that does that thing when he half-smiles.

She looks back at me.

She raises one eyebrow.

She says, in the voice of a woman who has spent nine months not being looked at the way I have just been looked at, in the precise way I have just been looked at, by a man who has — I have to admit it — the bone structure of a man you would buy a magazine for: Sure.

I eat the soup dumplings standing at the counter, very slowly, watching Pretzel watch me from the floor.

I do not, after the soup dumplings, get any work done.

I open my laptop. I close it. I open my phone. I close it. I open the fridge. I close it. I pick up the novel I have been meaning to read for two weeks, by a writer I respect, who has written carefully about heartbreak and recovery, and I read four pages without absorbing any of them, because the four pages are competing with the half-smile of a man in a charcoal coat in an elevator on the way to thirty.

I put the novel down.

I take Pretzel out for one last walk.

In the lobby, on the way out, Marcus is at the desk. He looks up. He clocks me with the small careful neutrality of a man who has been told, somewhere in the last ten minutes, that I will be of professional interest.

"Evening, Ms. Wright."

"Evening, Marcus."

"Will you be needing anything sent up tonight."

"No."

"Very good, Ms. Wright."

I walk Pretzel around the block.

It is, on the walk back, that I notice — high above me, against the December dark — the warm rectangle of the light on the thirtieth floor. It is the only floor in the building that is lit at this hour, on the side I am looking at. It is, somehow, a very lonely light.

I think, with the small clean clarity my heart has not given me in nine months:

Oh, Hadley. Be careful.

I think, half a second after:

Oh, Hadley. You are not, you are not, you are not going to be careful.

I take Pretzel inside. I go to bed.

In the bed, with the city quiet at the window, in the long pale wash of the Tribeca streetlights on the ceiling, I lie awake for an hour and a half, and I think about a man whose hand was very large and very dry and very warm and very gone before I had the chance to feel it.

I sleep, eventually.

I dream of an elevator that does not stop.

Chapter 2

The Doorman

The card comes down with the morning coffee.

I do not, when Marcus phones up at 7:50 from the lobby, expect either coffee or a card. I expect — by the small Pavlovian conditioning of nine months of New York friends checking on me — that he is about to tell me a delivery has come for Pretzel from one of my best friend's cousins who keeps sending the Pomeranian increasingly upmarket toys. What he says instead is: "Ms. Wright. There is a coffee and a card at the lobby desk for you. From the penthouse. Shall I send Eric up with them, or would you like to come down."

I sit, in the borrowed bed, in the Parsons sweatshirt I have decided to never take off, with my phone at my ear.

"The penthouse," I say.

"Yes, ma'am."

"Marcus."

"Yes, Ms. Wright."

"Has the penthouse done this before."

A pause. Marcus is not the kind of doorman who answers a question with a story. Marcus is the kind of doorman who answers a question with the small unembellished sentence that contains everything you need to know.

"Not since I have been at this desk, Ms. Wright."

I close my eyes.

I open my eyes.

"Send Eric up," I say. "Thank you, Marcus."

"Very good, ma'am."

Eric is the runner. Eric is twenty-three and has the careful bored politeness of a man who has spent two years in this lobby waiting for a real opportunity to do something with his English degree from Wesleyan. He brings up, at 7:54, a paper cup of coffee in a corrugated sleeve and a small ivory card-stock envelope on a polished steel tray.

I take the coffee.

I take the card.

I tip Eric ten dollars because his salary is what it is and because he has been very correct, in every small interaction we have had, and because the tip is, in any case, a piece of the apology I owe him for having any of this happen at the start of his Friday.

I close the door.

I look at the cup.

I take the lid off.

It is, on the first sip, exactly the coffee I ordered yesterday at La Mercerie around the corner. Not the same chain — a different chain, but the same drink. A small flat white with the espresso pulled long, half-and-half steamed but not microfoamed, no sugar. I had said, very specifically, please don't microfoam it, I'd rather it be flat than aerated, to the barista, who had given me the look young baristas give you when you say things that have, since the rise of latte art, become heretical. He had pulled it the way I asked. He had said here you go, Hadley, because I had used my name on the app.

I look at the cup in my hand.

There is no coffee shop on Worth or Hudson or Greenwich that pulls a flat white this exact way. There is exactly one chain in Tribeca that does — Maman, two blocks over, which I have not been to in three days because I went to La Mercerie instead.

He has, in other words, paid attention.

I open the card.

The card is the size of a business card on heavy cream stock. The handwriting is plain and small and entirely his — no one who wrote like this got it ghostwritten by an assistant.

> If you don't want me to do this again, leave the cup in the lobby and I won't. > Damon

I sit down on the borrowed kitchen barstool with the coffee in my hand and the card in my hand and Pretzel attempting, at my feet, to attract my attention by being adorably in the way.

I think: I am thirty-one years old, and I am a fashion editor, and I have, in my time, been pursued in various clumsy ways by various clumsy men, and not one of them has, in pursuit of me, ever sent down a card that comes with an opt-out.

I think: This man.

I think: Hadley. Be careful. He is the kind of careful that is, in another light, a kind of hunting.

I think: He is also possibly just a widower who has decided to date you. Don't make him a villain. Find out which.

I drink the coffee.

I do not, after I have drunk the coffee, leave the cup in the lobby.

I rinse it, on instinct, and set it on the drying rack on the borrowed counter. I am not sure, in the moment, whether that is a gesture or a habit. I think — I will admit it — that I am not yet entirely sure which side of the question I am, in fact, on.

I get dressed for work.

I go down at 8:35.

In the lobby, Marcus tips two fingers off his hat at me as I cross to the doors.

"Ms. Wright."

"Marcus."

"He's already gone. He left at quarter past seven."

I stop.

I look at Marcus.

Marcus has, in his small unembellished neutrality, the look of a man who has decided that he is, in this matter, on my side, and that the small assistance he can offer me is the small assistance of an additional piece of information.

"He's a good man, Ms. Wright. For what that's worth, coming from a doorman."

"Thank you, Marcus."

"You are welcome."

I go out onto Worth Street with the warm-air-blast of the building behind me and the cold of the morning hitting me in the face, and I walk the eight blocks to the Nine office in the West Village with my hands in my coat pockets and my coffee — a different coffee, an inferior coffee, the third coffee I will have today — paid for at La Mercerie by the side door.

I do not, in any of those blocks, decide what I am going to do.

I do not, all day at Nine, decide either.

I do not decide at the styling check at three, or at the editor's meeting at four, or at the long telephone call with the publicist for the cover talent at five.

I decide on the walk home.

I decide at the corner of Worth and Greenwich, in the way one decides things in this city — by walking far enough that the indecision starts to feel embarrassing.

I decide that I will, this evening, get into the elevator. I will press fourteen. I will go to my floor. I will do my work. I will eat my Joe's Shanghai. I will walk my dog. I will sleep my sleep.

I will, however, before any of that, send up to thirty — via Marcus, via Eric, via whatever runner is available — a coffee at a similarly absurd hour tomorrow morning, with my own small ivory card.

I have not yet decided what the card will say.

I am thinking about it on the walk.

I am still thinking about it when I get into the lobby, and Marcus nods to me, and I go to the elevator, and the elevator doors open.

He is in the elevator.

He has just come down from thirty. He has, by the look of him, just come from work — the charcoal coat is the same coat, the briefcase is the same briefcase, the very faint citrus is the same citrus. He is holding the door open for me.

"Ms. Wright."

I get in.

He presses lobby. The doors close.

"You're going down," I say.

"Briefly."

"How briefly."

"I have a dinner in the East Village. I am, at the rate I am moving, ten minutes late for it."

The elevator descends.

"Mr. Kade."

"Damon."

"Damon. The coffee was very kind."

"I'm glad you drank it."

"How did you know which kind."

He looks at me over his shoulder — half-smile, half something — and says: "Marcus."

"Marcus."

"Marcus has, for four years, been a great help to me on a number of small logistical matters. He is, I should tell you, also extremely loyal to you. He has told me three things about you in the last forty-eight hours and the third of them, this morning, was that I should not, under any circumstances, send up anything I would not put my name to, because Ms. Wright is a woman who pays attention to the small things. He was a great help on the coffee."

"He gave you my order."

"He gave me your order. He also gave me the speech he would give me, if I were ever to behave badly. He was very clear that the speech ended in 'Don't.'"

"Marcus is a good man."

"He is."

The elevator reaches the lobby. The doors open.

He steps out.

I step out.

In the lobby, with Marcus looking at us politely from the desk, he turns to me.

"Hadley Wright."

"Damon Kade."

"I am late for a dinner. I would not, otherwise, be leaving you in a lobby. May I, when I have recovered from the dinner, ask you if you would have dinner with me on Saturday."

I look at him.

I look at Marcus, who is not looking at me.

I look at Pretzel, who is upstairs, and who, in absentia, somehow has an opinion about this.

"Yes," I say. "You may ask me. I make no promises about the answer."

"That is a fair price for the question."

He gives me a small half-smile that is, this time, more than a half-smile.

He goes out into the street.

I stand in the lobby of the Whitcomb with Marcus at the desk and the doorbell about to ring and a small clean clarity in my chest that I have not felt in nine months and twenty-three days, and I think:

Hadley Wright. You are in trouble.

I think:

Hadley Wright. You are about to let it happen.

I go upstairs.

I do not, that night, write a card back.

I do not, that night, sleep very well either.

Above me, somewhere in the dark of the city, the warm rectangle of the thirtieth-floor window goes on burning.

Chapter 3

The Gym at Midnight

I cannot sleep.

This is, by Friday night of my first proper week at 14C, not new information. I have not slept properly since I found the receipts from the Four Seasons San Francisco in Tom's overnight bag in the middle of August, and the version of insomnia I had then has become, by now, the version of insomnia that does not even wake me up — I just don't go to sleep. I lie in the borrowed bed in the small lavender wash of the streetlight and Pretzel snores on the pillow next to me and the city is, around the bedroom, the city.

At eleven thirty I get up.

I put on a sports bra and a pair of leggings and an old grey hoodie. I take the elevator down to the gym.

The Whitcomb's gym is on the second floor and is a piece of small irrational luxury that the building uses to justify its rents. It has a wall of windows facing a small Tribeca courtyard. Two treadmills. A Pilates reformer. A row of weights. Mood lighting, fresh towels, a Dyson air purifier humming on low, a Sonos playing whatever the night-shift attendant decided would be tolerable to people unable to sleep. At eleven-forty on a Friday, it is empty.

I get on the second treadmill.

I do not, at first, run. I walk.

I am four minutes into the walk when the gym door opens.

Damon Kade comes in.

He is wearing grey sweatpants and a black long-sleeve and old running shoes that have, by their condition, been on his feet at this gym at this hour for some time. He does not look surprised to see me.

He does not look anything but what he looks. He looks like a man who has decided he is not going to make a fuss about a coincidence.

"Hadley."

"Damon."

"This is the third night this week I haven't slept. Don't say anything about it. I'll embarrass myself."

"Get on the treadmill."

He gets on the first treadmill.

He sets it to a six-minute mile.

I set mine to a seven-minute mile.

We run.

We do not talk. There is no version of this scene in which we would talk — both of us know it, without saying so, the way people who have lost things and have been polite about it for too long always know it. The thing we are both doing in the gym at midnight is not the kind of thing that gets talked through. It is the kind of thing that gets outrun.

I run for thirty-eight minutes.

He runs alongside me for thirty-eight minutes.

He matches my pace down to mine, after the first kilometer. I notice, in my peripheral vision, his shoulders settle. His breath comes the way mine comes — clean, four-count, the kind of rhythm a person uses to keep their own brain at arm's length.

At thirty-six minutes I cool down. He cools down. We are, at the cool-down, both walking and breathing and red in the face and not making eye contact in the polite, slightly-embarrassed way of people who have just done a thing together that was, in retrospect, an embarrassingly intimate thing to have done with a stranger.

I step off.

I towel off.

He steps off.

He towels off.

He looks at me.

"Do you want a drink."

"At midnight."

"At twelve eighteen. There is, on thirty, a bottle of a particular Highland malt I bought in March of last year that I have not, since opening, drunk more than two glasses of. I would like a third glass and I would like to drink it not alone. You are welcome to have a Pellegrino if you don't want the whisky. There is a doorman, an elevator, and a Pomeranian who will know exactly how long you are gone. The window is twenty minutes."

It is, in its way, the most carefully consent-aware invitation I have ever received in my adult life.

"Twenty minutes," I say.

"Twenty minutes."

"All right."

We go up together in the elevator. I do not, in the elevator, look at him directly. He does not, in the elevator, look at me directly. We are still both red in the face. The elevator goes past fourteen. The elevator goes past twenty. The elevator reaches thirty.

The doors open onto a small private vestibule with a single dark wood door at the end.

He unlocks the door.

He stands aside.

I go in.

I have, in nine years at Nine, been in a lot of apartments in this city. I have shot in townhouses in the East Sixties. I have catered in a Calatrava-designed penthouse on the West Side. I have, two summers ago, attended a small private dinner for a very well-known designer in a duplex in Tribeca that had a Brâncuși in the entry.

I have never been in an apartment that looked like this one.

It is — three thousand square feet, which I know because everyone in this building knows what every other apartment in this building square-foots. The southern wall is glass. The view is the whole city — the Hudson on the right, the East River on the left, the spire of the Freedom Tower and the long arm of the Brooklyn Bridge and a hundred thousand small windows of other people's lives, lit up against the dark. The flooring is wide-plank oak, the walls a clean warm white. The furniture is two long sofas in a slate-grey wool, a single armchair in oxblood leather, a low oak coffee table, a single Calder mobile turning very slowly in the airflow above the kitchen island, and three pieces of art that I recognize immediately, by name, by artist, and which together cost the same as a small Connecticut town.

There are no photographs.

There are no framed photographs anywhere in the apartment.

That is the first thing I notice in the second sweep.

The second thing I notice is that the apartment is, despite the size of it, empty. Not in the obvious way. In the small interior way. As if the things in it have all been there a long time and nobody has, in some recent stretch of months, moved any of them.

He sees me see it.

He does not comment.

He goes to the kitchen — long marble island, the kind of stove that takes up two-thirds of the wall — and pours, into two small heavy crystal tumblers, two precise fingers of whisky each. He brings them across the room. He hands me one.

"Lagavulin sixteen."

"I know."

"You like Islay."

"I do."

"So do I."

We drink.

The whisky is — it is exactly what the whisky is. There is not a better whisky in the world to drink at twelve-twenty in the morning after thirty-eight minutes on a treadmill with a man you do not, yet, know.

He sits on one of the slate sofas. He gestures at the other one. I do not sit on the other one. I sit on the same one. Two and a half feet from him. I cross my legs. He turns half-toward me. The view is, behind us, the city.

"Tell me about Pretzel," he says.

I laugh — a small, surprised, real laugh.

"Pretzel," I say, "is a small dog with strong opinions. He belongs to my best friend Phoebe and her wife Margaux. Phoebe is from money — I should say, since you live in this building, her grandfather's apartment is where I'm staying — and Margaux is a violinist with the Met. Pretzel is theirs. They are in Lisbon for six months. They have given me the apartment in exchange for dog-sitting. He has, since I arrived, decided I am the lesser of three available custodians and that he will tolerate me with skepticism."

"How long have you been here."

"Five days."

"How long are you here for."

"Until April."

"And what do you do."

"I'm a fashion editor at Nine. I commission and edit the editorial features, the longer-form pieces — designer profiles, criticism, that side of the magazine. Not the e-commerce. Not the front-of-book. The actual writing."

"You're an editor."

"I am an editor."

"You're a writer."

I look at him over my glass.

"How did you go from one to the other."

"You wrote," he said, "the long piece in the September issue. The one about the small Italian leather-goods firm. The one with the line about the kind of leather that has gone soft in the right pockets."

I look at him.

I look at him for a long second.

"You read Nine?"

"I read everything. I read the Atlantic, I read Nine, I read the Times, I read Foreign Affairs, I read Bon Appétit. I read in long uninterrupted blocks because if I don't I will, in the middle of any meeting, find a way to think about my dead wife. I read your September piece in February of this year and I have, since then, read your byline." He pauses. "It is not, for the avoidance of doubt, why I got off the elevator at fourteen."

"What is."

He looks at me over his own glass.

He sets the glass down.

He puts his hand on the sofa cushion between us. He does not move it closer.

"I got off the elevator at fourteen," he said, "because I saw you in the lobby with the dog and the soup dumplings and your hood half-up, and you laughed at something Marcus said as you went past the desk, and I thought — for the first time in eighteen months — I want to know what that woman was laughing at. I have not, in eighteen months, wanted to know what anything was. I thought, in the elevator, if I do not get her name now, I will be a man on this elevator with her for six months and at no point in those six months will I have her name. So I got off the elevator and I asked you for it." He picked up the whisky again. "That is the long answer. The short answer is: because, Ms. Wright, you laughed."

I do not, for a long minute, say anything.

I drink the whisky.

The whisky is good.

"Damon."

"Yes."

"I am, in case there is any confusion about it, a person you should not get involved with right now."

"I had assumed."

"My last boyfriend had a wife in San Francisco. He visited her, on weekends, on what he told me were client trips. I found out by finding the receipts."

His face — the careful settled face — went absolutely still.

"Hadley."

"I know."

"That is — I am sorry. That is a particular cruelty."

"It is."

He does not move.

He says, after a long beat: "I have not, in any of my reading of your byline, been the kind of person who would have done that to you, Hadley. I will not, in any of the next months, be the kind of person who would have done that to you. I will demonstrate it. I am not asking anything of you yet. I am, for the avoidance of doubt, asking nothing more of you tonight than the company in which I have just had a third glass of a malt I haven't been able to drink alone since March." He pauses. "If you would like to have dinner with me on Saturday, you should tell me on the elevator on the way back to your floor. If you would not, we will be neighbours who say good morning at the lobby and who occasionally run on adjacent treadmills, and I will not, ever, do anything else."

I look at him.

I look at the window. The Brooklyn Bridge. The dark river. The small windows.

"I'll tell you on the elevator," I say.

He smiles. The smile is, this time, the real one.

He walks me to the door. He calls the elevator. We get in. He presses fourteen. We descend.

At fourteen, the doors open.

I step out.

I turn.

I look at him.

"Saturday," I say.

He inclines his head.

"Saturday."

The elevator doors close.

I stand in the hallway in my running clothes and a hoodie that has gone half-damp at the back, and I think, with the small clean clarity my heart has been giving me since Wednesday:

Hadley. You are in trouble.

I think, half a second after:

You are also, for the first time since August, alive.

I walk down to 14C. I let myself in. Pretzel meets me at the door, indignant.

I get into the shower.
